Abstract—Dopant freeze-out severely increases the bulk re-
sistance of cryogenic bulk CMOS transistors by up to 106
× at
4.2 K compared to room temperature. This work describes, for
the first time in the literature, how this increased bulk resistance
introduces a memory effect in the latch of dynamic comparators,
which leads to hysteresis. To measure this hysteresis reliably in
the presence of noise, a statistical characterization procedure
is developed. For a 40 nm bulk CMOS strongARM comparator
with an input-referred noise voltage of 348 µVRMS, a hysteresis
voltage >898 µV is measured at 6 K, substantially deteriorating
the precision. Therefore, this paper introduces a triple tail
comparator with capacitive over-neutralization to increase the
preamplification gain, suppressing the hysteresis >6× to only
141 µV.
Index Terms—Cryo-CMOS, dopant freeze-out, bulk resistance,
memory effect, hysteresis, strongARM, dynamic comparator
I. INTRODUCTION
CRYO-CMOS has recently gained much attention thanks
to emerging applications, such as quantum computing,
particle physics experiments, and gravitational wave detectors
[1], [2], [3], [4], [5], [6], [7]. To date, cryogenic character-
ization and modeling of modern CMOS processes primarily
focus on DC-characteristics [6], [7], [8]. However, these are
insufficient for analog-to-digital converters (ADCs), which
contain many switching circuits such as dynamic comparators,
since any inaccuracy due to transient effects in the comparator
affects the full ADC performance. Still, in the literature, the
characterization of cryogenic comparators is limited to a noise
analysis of a strongARM comparator for a 7-bit ADC [9].
This paper shows, for the first time in the literature, that
cryogenic bulk CMOS comparators such as the prevalent
strongARM comparator in Fig. 1 suffer from hysteresis in
their latch. This hysteresis is induced by a severe increase
(106
×) of the bulk resistance due to dopant freeze-out [6],
[10], [11]. The presented work provides additional insights
into cryogenic bulk CMOS behavior and advances the state-of-
the-art in three ways. Firstly, it describes in detail how dopant
freeze-out introduces hysteresis in cryogenic comparators.
This article focuses on dynamic comparators, but the same
concepts can be applied to assess the impact of dopant freeze-
out on any cryogenic circuit with fast switching transients.

II. DOPANT FREEZE-OUT INDUCED HYSTERESIS
The strongARM (SA) comparator [9], [12] in Fig. 1 is a
popular topology thanks to its fast, energy-efficient, and robust
operation. It comprises a capacitive preamplifier (M0-1) and a
latch (M2-3). During the reset phase (CLK = VDD), the pream-
plifier and latch outputs are discharged to VSS through the
reset switches (S1-2). In the comparison phase (CLK = VSS),
the tail transistor M0 is turned on, gradually charging VDP
and VDN to VDD at a slightly different rate, which generates
some gain before the latch turns on. After the comparison
phase, the next reset phase should erase all information from
the previous decision. At room temperature, a proper reset is
easily guaranteed by correctly sizing S1-2.
At cryogenic temperatures, however, dopant freeze-out
severely increases the substrate resistance by up to 106
× at
4.2 K compared to room temperature [6], [10], [11]. This leads
to a large and unpredictable bulk resistance RB, as highlighted
in Fig. 1. Such a high resistance leaves the bulk vulnerable
for capacitive coupling of the latch outputs through the drain-
bulk capacitance CDB. The bulk voltage, and consequently

Since foundry models do not support cryogenic tempera-
tures, the effects of dopant freeze-out are emulated by placing
an additional large RB between the bulk terminal and VDD or
VSS. The transient simulations with room temperature models
and an RB of 15 MΩ in Fig. 2 show that the bulk voltages
of M2-3 are heavily disturbed by the large voltage swing at
VOP/N and VDP/N. Even at a low clock frequency fclk of only
10 MHz, the bulk voltages cannot recover to their DC-values
by the end of the reset phase. When the previous comparator
decision was positive (OUTk-1 = 1), VB3P will be lower than
VB3N at the start of the next comparison, while VB2P will be
lower than VB2N. Hence, M3P will have a higher VT than M3N,
whereas M2P will have a lower VT than M2N. This increases
the probability of a positive decision during the next com-
parison. Due to the symmetrical topology of the comparator,
a previous negative decision (OUTk-1 = 0) will analogously
increase the probability of a negative decision during the next
comparison. Consequently, the increased bulk resistance at
cryogenic temperatures could introduce a noticeable hysteresis
in the latch. The swing at VDP/N will also disturb the bulk of the
input pair, but since both VDP and VDN have the same swing
from VDD to VSS, this is mainly a common-mode disturbance.
Hence, it does not significantly affect the next decision.
As the freeze-out induced hysteresis favors the same deci-
sion as the previous one, it can be modeled by an input-referred
voltage source VHYS whose sign depends on the previous
decision, as shown in Fig. 3. Simulations show that VHYS may
rise substantially above the noise level of 350 µVRMS for bulk
resistances above 10 MΩ or clock frequencies above 10 MHz,
so that the precision of the SA comparator is bounded by
VHYS instead of noise. Such a large hysteresis can introduce
significant errors in an ADC. For example, in a successive-
approximation-register (SAR) ADC, VHYS results in a noise-
like precision degradation, as illustrated in Fig. 4. The corre-

III. PROPOSED TRIPLE TAIL COMPARATOR WITH
CAPACITIVE OVER-NEUTRALIZATION
Since the freeze-out induced hysteresis originates in the
latch, a high preamplification gain can effectively suppress
VHYS. However, this gain is limited in the conventional SA
comparator because it comprises only one preamplification
stage. Prior art multi-stage comparators usually include half-
latches in the preamplifier to increase the gain [13], [14].
However, these extra half-latches also suffer from the freeze-
out induced hysteresis, nullifying the benefit of the additional
preamplification gain.
Therefore, this work introduces the triple tail compara-
tor with capacitive over-neutralization (TTCO) in Fig. 5. Its
preamplifier consists of two stages, and the second stage
(M2) adopts the cross-coupled capacitors CN. These capacitors
boost the preamplification gain by overcompensating the gate-
drain capacitance of M2, as is typically done in RF and
mm-wave circuits [15], [16]. Cross-coupling these capacitors
creates a positive feedback path for differential signals from
the preamplifier outputs VXP/N to VDP/N. A larger CN provides
more positive feedback, leading to a noticeably higher gain, as
illustrated in the bottom-right graph of Fig. 5. In this way, the
preamplification gain is significantly larger for the proposed
TTCO comparator than for the conventional SA comparator.

In contrast to the half-latches in prior art multi-stage com-
parators, the positive feedback of the cross-coupled capacitors
does not cause full regeneration of the preamplifier outputs
VXP/N to VDD and VSS. Instead, both VXP and VXN are
precharged to VDD during the reset phase and discharged to
VSS during the comparison phase. Hence, the voltage swing at
the drains of M2 causes primarily a common-mode disturbance
of the bulk voltages, introducing negligible hysteresis. Conse-
quently, the TTCO comparator is considerably less susceptible
to freeze-out induced hysteresis than multi-stage comparators
with half-latches in their preamplifiers.
IV. MEASUREMENT SETUP
To demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed capacitive
over-neutralization, a chip containing an SA and three TTCO
comparators (CN of 2 fF, 6 fF and 10 fF) was manufactured
in a 40 nm bulk CMOS technology. All comparators were
sized for similar noise and offset. To mimic the cryogenic
I-V characteristics in simulations, the BSIM4 mobility and VT
model parameters were adapted based on [6], so that foundry
statistical data can be reused for approximate Monte Carlo
simulations [7]. To compensate for the higher VT at cryogenic
temperatures [6], [7], [8], all transistors are implemented as
low-VT (LVT) core devices. Furthermore, all comparators
employ a PMOS input pair to counteract the higher impact
of 1/f-noise at cryogenic temperatures [17]. To reduce RB as
much as possible, a double row of bulk contacts is placed as
close to the latch as minimally allowed by the foundry design
rules, as shown in the example layouts of Fig. 6.
The measurement setup and chip micrograph are depicted
in Fig. 7. The chip is mounted on a PCB and installed
in a Montana Instruments s200 cryostation for cryogenic
characterization. The samples are thermalized by fixing them
directly on a gold-plated copper heat sink with silver paint
[7]. The minimal achievable platform temperature of this setup
is 6 K. The input signals are low-pass filtered to remove the

VI. MEASUREMENT RESULTS
For the noise measurements, the clock is set to a low
frequency of 100 kHz, resulting in the output distributions
shown in Fig. 9. The σnoise derived from these CDFs serves as
an input for the hysteresis characterization in Fig. 10. The VHYS
of all four comparators is characterized for three samples and
clock frequencies from 100 kHz to 50 MHz. At 100 kHz, VHYS
is negligible. However, around 5 MHz, the SA comparator
has a VHYS above 850 µV, which is almost 3σnoise. At higher
frequencies, VHYS cannot be characterized accurately anymore
due to the high sensitivity of the inverse error function when
the autocorrelation gets too close to 1. This means that VHYS is
even higher at these high frequencies. In contrast, the TTCO
comparators show a noticeably lower VHYS. The extra stages
and the capacitive over-neutralization provide a significantly
higher preamplification gain, which successfully suppresses
VHYS.

VII. CONCLUSION
The freeze-out induced increase of the bulk resistance
jeopardizes a proper reset of the latch in cryogenic bulk CMOS
comparators. This introduces hysteresis, favoring the same
decision as the previous one. This work developed a statistical
characterization procedure to reliably measure the hysteresis
in the presence of noise, showing that hysteresis severely
degrades the precision of the popular strongARM comparator.
In contrast, the proposed TTCO comparator effectively sup-
presses the hysteresis in a simple and robust way by boosting
the preamplification gain using capacitive over-neutralization.
The prototype TTCO comparator with a CN of 10 fF has a
hysteresis voltage of only 141 µV, which stays well below its
noise voltage of 237 µVRMS. Hence, the TTCO comparator
maintains high precision in the cryogenic environment up to
clock frequencies well above 10 MHz.
