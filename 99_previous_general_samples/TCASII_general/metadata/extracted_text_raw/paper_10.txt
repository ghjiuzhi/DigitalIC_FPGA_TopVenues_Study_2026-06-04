A Lightweight and Hardware-Efficient NTT FPGA
Accelerator for FHE Applications
Moonis Amir Zaman, Muhammad Bilal Khan, Waqar Ahmad, Ayesha Khalid, Máire O’Neill
Abstract—The Number Theoretic Transform (NTT) and its
inverse (INTT) are pivotal operations in Fully Homomorphic
Encryption (FHE) schemes to facilitate efficient polynomial
multiplication over modular rings. We propose a compact and
hardware-efficient NTT/INTT architecture for FHE systems with
a novel twiddle factor generator (TFG) for Radix-2 multi-path
delay commutator (MDC) designs, saving up to 97.2% of on-
chip memory by storing the twiddle factors (TF) for the initial 11
stages (for log2(N) = 15 to 17) and only the TF bases for on-the-
fly generation in the remaining stages. DSP-efficient multipliers
via non-standard tiling are designed to reduce DSP utilization
as compared to standard tiling, without sacrificing performance.
Benchmarking on comparable Xilinx FPGAs reveals that our
design is the most compact, along with a significant efficiency ad-
vantage with up to 2.72× reduction in average area-time product
(ATP) and up to 2.57× increase in throughput-per-equivalent-
LUT (TPE) compared to state-of-the-art NTT designs. In various
configurations, the architecture maintains lower BRAM and DSP
utilization, and better overall efficiency, making it a scalable and
efficient solution for real-world FHE deployments.
Index Terms—Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE), Number
Theoretic Transform (NTT), Twiddle Factor Generation
I. INTRODUCTION
Polynomial arithmetic, in particular polynomial multipli-
cation, is crucial to modern cryptographic schemes such as
Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) and Post-Quantum
Cryptography (PQC) [1]. Polynomial multiplication tends to
be a bottleneck operation in these schemes. The Number
Theoretic Transform (NTT) comes into play here, reducing
the complexity from O(n2
) to O(n log2 n), making it a must-
have component of FHE hardware designs.
There are two commonly used pipelined architectures for
NTT, namely the single-path delay feedback (SDF) and multi-
path delay commutator (MDC) architectures, with their respec-
tive performance-resource trade-offs. Different cryptographic
schemes use a range of polynomial degrees (N) and modulus
lengths, e.g., FHE generally uses N values from 215
to 217
with bit widths (log2(q)) of 50 to 64 bits. The reported work
either optimizes NTT for specific parameter sets [N,log2(q)],
limiting its usability, or are run-time configurable for different
parameters [2], resulting in large resource overhead. In con-
trast, compile-time configurable designs (parameters provided

architectures. While there is limited work in the literature
for architectures supporting high-degree polynomial param-
eters, essential for next-generation cryptographic primitives
like FHE, most of the reported work has large hardware
overheads and high usage of memory due to twiddle factors
and DSPs used in the standard integer multipliers implemented
on FPGAs. Most of these reported designs precalculate and
store the twiddle factors (TF) in memory [3]–[5] and majority
do not support high-degree polynomials. For higher-order
polynomials, this precalculation can contribute to a significant
memory overhead.
In this paper, we present a compact and hardware-efficient
NTT hardware architecture that can accommodate high-order
polynomials from 215
to 217
. Our design can be configured at
compile-time for different parameter sets. Our contributions
are two-fold: First, to alleviate the high BRAM usage as-
sociated with storing precomputed twiddle factors (TF), we
employ a hybrid approach of using some precomputed TF
bases in memory and computing others on the fly. To reach
an optimal split in this context, we carry out an exhaustive
design space exploration to reach the optimal number of
precomputed / on-the-fly stages to reach the most area-time
product (ATP) efficient split, which we believe to be the
first reported optimal twiddle factor generator (TFG) design
for Radix-2 MDC architectures since most of the reported
TFG designs for NTT are iterative architectures [2], [6], [7].
Second, since the generation of TFs requires additional mul-
tipliers, we also propose non-standard DSP tiling multiplier
designs for various bit widths, that help reduce DSP usage
compared to standard tiling based DSP multiplier designs used
commonly in the literature. To our knowledge, we are the
first to implement these DSP based optimizations on NTT
architectures. Our experimental results show that we achieve
91.7% ∼ 97.2% reduction in on-chip memory for TFs, 1.15×
∼ 2.72× reduction in average area-time product (ATP) and
1.25× ∼ 2.57× increase in throughput-per-equivalent-LUT
(TPE) compared to the state-of-the-art NTT designs.

Two well-known approaches, Cooley-Tukey (CT) and

Twiddle Factor Generation: Twiddle factors (TF) are con-
stants (ψ) used in the calculation of NTT/INTT. The higher the
order of N, larger the number of TFs needed, e.g., for a unified
NTT design with N = 217
with log2(q) = 64 bits, 262144 TFs
are needed since (218
×64)/8 = (262144×64)/8 ≈ 2.10 MB
of data. To reduce on-chip memory, these TFs can be generated
on-the-fly by executing the following recursive equation with
a known initial value of ψ, [9]:

Since most NTT butterfly dataflows require TFs in bit-
reversed order, additional TF bases need to be stored as well,
complicating the hardware design. Additionally, the generation
of the next TF requires feedback from the previous output of
the modular multiplier, which can result in additional latency
for pipelined architectures, as modular multipliers typically
take more than one cycle to execute. Reported work, including
[3], [5], use precomputed TFs in memory, resulting in high
memory overhead for large N. Other reported work, including
[2], [6], [7], implement TFG modules but are limited to
iterative NTT architectures. To the best of our knowledge, this
work is the first reported optimal TFG for MDC architectures.
MDC architectures: Also called pipelined architectures,
use only one butterfly unit (BU) for each stage of NTT/INTT,
while iterative designs can have multiple BUs in parallel per
stage, as shown in Fig. 1. These architectures take two input
polynomial coefficients and generate two coefficients every
cycle once their pipeline is full. Two FIFO (memory) units and
a commutator switch (SW) are used at each stage to reorder
the intermediate coefficients according to the required offset
pattern for the next stage. The depth of the FIFO units depends
on the formula [3]: d = N
2s+2 , where s = 0, 1, . . . , log2(N)−1.
III. PROPOSED OPTIMIZATIONS FOR NTT/INTT
A. Twiddle Factor Generator (TFG)
The first stage (BU0) requires only two TFs, one each for
NTT and INTT, while for subsequent stages the required TFs
increase in powers of two. To reduce the on-chip memory for
storing these TFs, we can generate most of them on-the-fly
by storing a few TF bases and employing modular multipliers
(MM). We aim to design a generator that does not require
any feedback from the MM output to generate the next TF;

The algorithm takes as input the number of initial stages to
be stored in memory, STORED, and the total number of stages,
LOGN. It also takes a precomputed table of N twiddle factors,
ψtable, in which each TF is multiplied by the Montgomery
factor, Rw, so that no correction operation is required later
in the hardware. The output consists of two ROM arrays,
ROM_G1 and ROM_G2, which store the bases for the two
inputs to the modular multipliers for each stage from STORED
to LOGN − 1. The algorithm iterates over each stage, factors
2s
into two components f1 and f2 to minimize their sum
(MFS), and calculates the required address bits for each ROM.
It then populates the ROMs with the appropriate TFs in bit-
reversed index order (BitRev), ensuring that the generated TFs
are correctly aligned for use in the NTT/INTT computations.

LUTs, which may increase the design area if implemented at
every stage. Fig. 2 shows the MDC architecture with the TFG,
where the first three stages store all TFs in ROM.

B. DSP-Efficient Multipliers
The integer multiplier is a crucial component of NTT/INTT.
Furthermore, the on-the-fly generation of TFs necessitates
additional multipliers, leading to increased DSP utilization. A
common technique for integer multiplication is the standard
tiling (ST) approach [11], employed also by [3], that parti-
tions the input operands into smaller chunks. The DSP48E1
multiplier in 7-series FPGAs is a 25×18 bits signed multiplier.
To emulate unsigned multiplication, the MSB of each operand
IEEE TRANSACTIONS ON CIRCUITS AND SYSTEMS 4
is set to 0, effectively performing 24×17 unsigned multiplica-
tion. Similarly, the DSP48E2 in Ultrascale+ FPGAs supports
27 × 18 signed and 26 × 17 unsigned multiplication. This
approach of using unsigned numbers avoids any logic required
for signed to unsigned number conversions, that can potentially
reduce the maximum frequency of the overall design.
Fig. 4(a) and (e) show the standard tiling configurations for
DSP48E1 and DSP48E2, respectively. In Fig. 4(a), for a 52 ×
52 bit multiplication, the first three DSPs of the last column
perform 4 × 17 bit multiplications each and the final DSP
performs a 4 × 1 bit multiplication, which is not an efficient
use of those DSPs. Similar issues can be observed for other bit
sizes such as 36, 54, and 64 (mainstream bit widths of FHE
accelerators), as well as in Fig. 4(e). Hence, we propose more
efficient DSP-mapping techniques for various bit ranges.
Our mapping algorithm dynamically partitions input
operands (a × b) based on the bit width of the modulus
(log2(q)) and the DSP dimensions available in the target FPGA
(24 × 17 or 26 × 17). It determines the optimal DSP count
by analyzing operand coverage in both width and height,
ensuring full multiplication representation without redundancy.
Operands are segmented into DSP-aligned chunks, and partial
products from each chunk pair are aligned via left shifts and
accumulated by a carry-save adder (CSA) tree to reduce carry
propagation delay. A final two-operand addition produces the
product. This structured approach minimizes DSP usage while
preserving full precision and high operating frequency across
varying operand sizes.

IV. HARDWARE IMPLEMENTATION RESULTS
We implemented our proposed designs on various Xilinx
FPGAs using Xilinx Vivado 2019.1. We configured our TFG to
precomputed 11 stages, as discussed in Section III-A, and used
a unified-NWC architecture. Table I shows the Post-Place-and-
Route (Post-PAR) results comparison between different NTT
designs in the literature for large polynomial degree sizes. We
calculated the throughput (TP) as done in [2]. We calculated
the throughput-per-equivalent-LUT (TPE), where the equiva-
lent LUT (EqL) for area utilization is calculated as done in [6].
The area-time product (ATP, ref. Section III-A) to determine
hardware efficiency is also computed. Note that we used the
average of the performance metrics for comparison, as the
NTT/INTT operations are used for many inputs consecutively,
in real world FHE deployments.
For N = 215
, log2(q) = 64, we compared our design to
[3], which is a state-of-the-art pipelined NTT architecture,
which can be configured as SDF or MDC. We compared our
work to a unified MDC configuration of this work. Our design
achieves 1.33× better average ATP and 1.35× equivalent LUT,
along with 1.35× better average TPE. Moreover, we use 3.03×
fewer BRAMs due to our TFG, at the expense of 1.20× more
DSPs, whereas [3] stores all its TFs in memory and therefore
requires fewer multipliers overall than our work.
For N = 216
, log2(q) = 52, we compared our work to [3]
and two iterative NTT architectures [6], [7]. Compared to [3],
our work uses 1.18× more DSPs due to on-the-fly computation
of TFs, however it uses 3.16× fewer BRAMs. Both [6], [7]
generate TFs on the fly, however, our work utilizes the lowest
number of DSPs and BRAMs. Since iterative architectures
can be parallelized, they are faster as compared to pipelined
architectures. While [6] and [7] take nearly 2× fewer average
clock cycles, and 1.69× and 1.58× lower average latency,
respectively, our design outperforms them in average ATP and
TPE. Specifically, it achieves 1.56× and 1.49× better ATP, and
1.60× and 1.50× better TPE, respectively.

For N = 216
, log2(q) = 60 − 64, we compared our
design to [2], [3], [5], [12]. Our architecture achieves the
best average ATP from 1.15× to 1.53× better than the other
designs, while also achieving 1.25× to 1.53× better average
TPE. Furthermore, our design has the lowest BRAM usage.
[2] uses Radix-24
BUs which processes more coefficients in
parallel as compared to our Radix-2 BUs, hence they have high
throughput however, our TPE is still higher. [12] utilities the
fewest number of DSPs, however, it has the highest number
of average clock cycles resulting in the longest latency. [5]
achieves very low latency despite comparatively lower number
of DSPs, however, it uses the highest number of BRAMs
resulting in 1.15× and 1.25× worse average ATP and TPE,
respectively, than our design.
Finally, for N = 217
and log2(q) = 60 − 62, we compared
our work to [2], [13], [14]. [2] can be configured for N = 216
or N = 217
at run time; hence, their resource usage is identical
for each parameter. Our work achieves a 1.27× higher average
ATP and 1.28× higher average TPE. Additionally, our design
is more compact as it uses 2.55× fewer equivalent LUTs and
1.44× fewer DSPs. [13] achieves better average ATP and TPE
than our work, however, it uses 1.46× more equivalent LUTs
and 1.07× more DSPs than our design. Compared to [14],
we achieve significant improvements of 2.72× and 2.57×
in average ATP and TPE, respectively, however, their work
supports the Residue Number System based FHE schemes
without need for reconfiguration when switching modulus.
V. CONCLUSION
This paper proposes novel optimization techniques for MDC
NTT/INTT architectures for FHE applications. We achieve a
reduction of up to 97.2% in memory utilization by employing a
novel TFG. Combined with DSP-efficient multiplier designs,
these optimizations deliver significantly better average ATP
and TPE, as compared to most reported literature. Future
work includes extending the TFG to support different radix
configurations of pipelined architectures, such as Radix-4, and
developing a generator for single-path delay feedback (SDF)
NTT architectures.
