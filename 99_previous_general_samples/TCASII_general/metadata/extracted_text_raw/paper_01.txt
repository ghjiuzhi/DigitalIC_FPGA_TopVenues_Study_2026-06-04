Watermarking-based Defense Mechanism in LFC of
Electricity Grids Compromised by Covert Attacks
Soroush Oshnoei, Mohammad Reza Aghamohammadi, Jalal Heidari, and Mohammad Hassan Khooban, Senior
Member, IEEE
Abstract—This paper focuses on improving the load frequency
control (LFC) performance of a multi-area power system com-
promised by covert attacks. In this regard, a novel detection tech-
nique based on the multiplicative watermarking and Luenberger
observer is developed to identify covert attacks applied to the
system. In the presented detection strategy, a special watermark
generator filter produces a dummy signal on the original signal
transmitted through the insecure communication channels. In
addition, there is a watermark equalization filter at the end of
the communication channel, which cancels out the impact of
the watermark signal, resulting in the recovery of the original
signal. A PI controller tuned by the deep reinforcement learning
algorithm and dependent on the Luenberger observer is utilized
as the secondary frequency control strategy to mitigate the
impacts of the convert attacks. The OPAL-RT real-time emulator-
based results show the proposed defense strategy successfully
detects and mitigates the covert attacks imposed on the system.
Index Terms—Load frequency control, covert attacks, multi-
plicative watermarking, deep reinforcement learning, Luenberger
observer, multi-area power system.
I. INTRODUCTION
AVariety of closed-loop control systems, such as load
frequency control (LFC) with geographically distributed
sensing and actuation components, is employed to operate
power systems [1]. Wide-area monitoring system-based ap-
plications are used to obtain the system’s real-time data under
different conditions [2], [3]. Automatic generation control
(AGC) service, as the most important section of the LFC
application, controls the frequency and tie-line power of elec-
tricity grids using remote data transmitted by communication
networks. Due to the presence of the AGC service in LFC
schemes, power systems are more susceptible to cyberattacks,
compromising the system’s stability [4], [5].
In recent years, cyber-security has become an increasingly
important topic for LFC systems, and a number of literature
have been accomplished to study the implications and design
countermeasures regarding different cyberattacks, including
denial-of-service [5], and false data injection [6], [7] attacks. In
[8], a survey on learning-based methods has been presented to
detect cyberattacks in communication network-based systems.

needed on stealthy attacks in LFC systems. The covert attack is
a stealth attack strategy that can severely compromise the sys-
tem’s performance without detection. Accordingly, this paper
studies the LFC performance of power systems compromised
by covert threats and proposes a defense mechanism against
these attacks.
Despite the presence of detection logic in the energy man-
agement systems of the LFC application, various stealthy
attacks with comprehensive plant dynamics knowledge have
remained undetected [9]. The covert attack is regarded as
one of those stealthy attacks capable of disruption and dis-
closure with a high level of model knowledge. When an
attacker generates control actions in the covert attack setup,
the controller does not detect abnormalities because the covert
agent removes the plant actuation effect by subtracting output
measurements from output responses. Moreover, the adversary
utilizes retained thresholds to reduce the number of false
alarms caused by disturbances and model uncertainties [10].
As a result, despite the discrepancies between the attacker’s
plant model and the real system, the covert adversary remains a
hidden cybernetic ally. A better description of the attack would
be that attackers first try to interfere with the network channel
between the controller and actuator to disrupt the power sys-
tem’s performance. After that, some carefully planned signals
are applied to the sensor or controller channel to eliminate
input attack effects [11]. In this way, even though the power
system may have been compromised, the measurement signals
remain the same, enabling these covert attacks undetected by
the normal detection techniques described above.
To date, only a few techniques have been developed to iden-
tify covert attacks. In [12], the authors proposed a detection
scheme consisting of model-based distributed estimators to
identify local covert attacks in linear interconnected systems.
A two-module distributed monitoring architecture comprising
a set of unknown input observers was proposed in [13] to
detect covert attacks applied to a DC microgrid considering
the ideality of the communication channel. An unsupervised
machine learning-based strategy named i-Forest was presented
in [14] to identify covert attacks imposed on communication
networks of power systems. The authors in [15] mentioned that
data-driven-based techniques are not useful when the bogus
measurement data vary slightly from learned standard use
patterns. The authors in [16] proposed an intrusion detection
scheme based on the GPS data and offline maps for intelligent
transportation systems compromised by covert attacks. In [11],
a recursive watermarking-based scheme was proposed to iden-
2
tify covert attacks in an industrial cyber-physical system. The
available identification strategies exhibit drawbacks, including
performance failure and identification delay.
Regarding the inability of available methods to encounter
stealth cyberattacks and the importance of preserving the grid’s
frequency in a permissible range under these attacks, this paper
investigates the LFC performance of an interconnected power
grid under stealth covert attacks. In this regard, covert attacks
are designed on a two-area LFC system. Moreover, a defense
mechanism based on dynamic multiplicative watermarking
technique is proposed to encounter covert attacks. In the
proposed defense mechanism, the multiplicative watermarking
technique, a Luenberger observer, and a detector are employed
to identify covert attacks. The multiplicative watermarking
superimposes a watermark signal on an original signal before
transmitting it over an insecure network. A reverse watermark
is also used after the watermark signal transits via an inse-
cure network, which mitigates the watermark’s effect under
any conditions. The motivation for containing the proposed
watermarking technique is to make covert attacks identifi-
able by having them cause a defective reconstruction of the
plant’s signals. Unlike all watermarking-based methods, the
multiplicative watermarking-based methods generally have a
higher capacity for embedding information than other meth-
ods. Moreover, adversaries in this method cannot identify or
copy the watermark parameters for estimating or removing the
watermark signal and compromising the signal integrity. A Lu-
enberger observer is also designed in the proposed mechanism
to provide the system’s state estimation and residual signal.
A deep reinforcement learning (DRL)-based PI controller
is also used in the AGC loop for frequency regulation of
the system compromised by covert attacks. The proposed
defense mechanism has the capability of coping with other
stealth cyberattacks and can be applied to different real-world
applications.

III. DESIGN OF COVERT ATTACKS ON THE LFC SYSTEM
Fig. 2 depicts the description of the covert attack on the
cyber-physical system. According to this figure, the attack
signal ua(t) is injected into the watermarked control signal to
disrupt the operation of the system, whereas the attack signal
ya(t) is injected into the watermarked output signals to remove
the impact of ua(t) on the measurement signal. The modeling
of covert attacks can be represented by:
(
ũ(t) = u(t) + ua(t)
ỹ(t) = y(t) − ya(t)
(3)
where ũ(t) and ỹ(t) are the control signal submitted to
the physical system and measurement output signal obtained
by the control center, respectively. The main hardship in
identifying such attacks is that the attacker can design ua(t)
and ya(t) to make the attack impact covert and not different
from the expected behavior. The attacker aims to configure
the disruption ua(t) such that the plant brings to some states
determined by the attacker. Simultaneously, the effect of the
attack ua(t) on the watermarked output signals yg is canceled
by ya(t). To this end, the attacker uses the dynamic system
given by (4):
( .
x̃i(t) = Aix̃i(t) + Biua(t)
ya(t) = Cix̃i(t)
(4)

where x̃i(t) shows the state vector of the dynamic system
used by the attacker (Attacker model of the plant in Fig. 2).
Since the disruption ua(t) applied by the attacker is arbitrary,
its features are generally unclear to defenders. Accordingly,
model (4) is satisfactory for defining a covert attacker. The
state-space equations of the system (1) under covert attacks
can be represented as follows:
( .

and output variables related to system (1) under covert attack.
ya,i(t) is generated by (4) to cancel the impact of the input
attack ua,i(t) against measurement signal.
Supposing that the adversary has a thorough knowledge
about the system (matrices Ai, Bi, Ci) and the attack begins
at time tc ≥ 0, the following conditions will illustrate the
covert attack’s stealthiness [12]. 1) If matrix A is Hurwitz,
the attacker can suitably design the ya(t) to produce the covert
attack as t → 0. 2) If the attacker determines x̃i(tc) = 0 then
the attack is covert for all instants and A.

VI. CONCLUSION
This research investigated the LFC performance of a two-
area power network against covert attacks. Accordingly, the
combination of the Luenberger observer and multiplicative
watermarking-based technique was proposed to detect covert
attacks. A DDPG-based PI controller was also employed as
the secondary controller to regulate the system’s frequency.
The real-time simulation results illustrated that the proposed
strategy successfully copes with covert attacks imposed on the
system and improves by the RMSE and ITAE indices at least
35% to other methods.
In future work, the proposed defense mechanism will be
