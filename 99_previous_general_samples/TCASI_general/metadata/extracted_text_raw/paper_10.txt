A 0.4–0.9 V Supply Voltage-Flexible Third-Order
Passive ΔΣ Modulator With Switched-Capacitor
Loop Filter Achieving 71.9 dB Peak SNDR
at 4 MHz Bandwidth
Jeroen Ponte , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Harijot Singh Bindra , Member, IEEE,
and Bram Nauta , Fellow, IEEE
Abstract— This paper describes the design and implementation
of a 4 MHz bandwidth 3rd-order Discrete-Time (DT) Passive
Delta-Sigma Modulator (PΔΣM), which avoids the use of supply
voltage-sensitive linear amplifiers. The Switched-Capacitor (SC)
operation and amplifier-less nature of its loop filter render the
proposed modulator highly supply voltage- and clock frequency-
flexible. To explain our design choices, we investigate two key
design challenges unique to PΔΣMs. Firstly, the noise criticality
of the quantizer ADC due to the lack of (voltage) gain in
the loop filter preceding it. Secondly, the inaccessibility of the
readily synthesizable and highly frequency-selective loop filter
frequency responses typically employed in ΔΣMs, caused by
the absence of linear amplifiers. We show that the noise-power
trade-off of the quantizer ADC in 1 bit P16Ms is the same
as that of the first integrator amplifier in Active ΔΣM. Next,
we cover the suitability of several passive filters for use in
PΔΣM loops. This leads to the selection of the voltage-sampled
Charge-Rotating IIR (CR-IIR) filter, for which we show ΔΣM-
oriented design equations, on which we base the design of
our 3rd-order DT PΔΣM. Measurement results on the resulting
prototype PΔΣM, implemented in GlobalFoundries 22 nm FD-
SOI CMOS, demonstrate that it achieves a 71.9 dB peak SNDR
and 4 MHz bandwidth while consuming 4.4 mW from a 0.9 V
supply and occupying 0.076mm2 of silicon area. The prototype
PΔΣM continues to operate at a 2.25× reduced supply voltage
of 0.4 V, albeit at a reduced bandwidth of 156.25 kHz, where it
achieves a peak SNDR of 62 dB while consuming 37.8 µW.
Index Terms— Analog-to-digital converter (ADC), discrete-
time delta-sigma (ΔΣ) modulation, passive switched-capacitor
filter, opampless, clock frequency-flexible, supply voltage-flexible,
CMOS, 22 nm FD-SOI.

II. KEY CHALLENGES IN THE DESIGN OF PASSIVE 16MS
The design of P16Ms presents two key challenges
not found in Active 16Ms. We begin our discussion by
considering Fig. 1, which shows a generalized model of
an N bit 16M. The loop filter is represented by two
(Continuous- or Discrete-Time) frequency-dependent transfer
functions HFF( f ) and HFB( f ). Typically, (different) HFF( f )
and HFB( f ) are implemented by (differently) coupling the
input and DAC feedback signals into a single, shared loop
filter. It can be demonstrated that a large (in-band) HFF( f )
magnitude, as typically present in active 16Ms, suppresses
the input-referred thermal noise contribution of the quantizer
ADC en,ADC [4].
Unfortunately, achieving such large voltage gain in a passive
(low-pass) loop filter is challenging. Techniques such as e.g.
capacitor stacking can be applied, but this requires additional
clock phases and the achievable voltage gain is limited by
parasitic loading [8]. Although the quantizer with its (implicit)
gain can compensate for the lack of gain in the loop filter,
it becomes a key input-referred P16M noise contributor.
As a result, the noise-power trade-off of the quantizer ADC
is the first key challenge in the design of P16M. We will
first discuss modeling of the (implicit) quantizer gain in
Section III-A. Continuing, Section III-B treats the fundamental
noise-power trade-off of a 1 bit quantizer ADC in a P16M,
and compares it with that of the first linear (integrator)
amplifier in Active 16M, which plays an analogous role.
We show that criticality of quantizer input noise in P16M is
not a direct bottleneck in achieving energy efficiencies similar
to their active counterparts.
The second key challenge involves achieving a high
modulator resolution in P16M, which requires high quantizer
gain, being related to the frequency-selectivity of their loop
filter as will be discussed in Section III. In Section IV we treat
how the omission of linear amplifiers constrains the loop filter
design space, disallowing the implementation of the highly-
selective frequency responses typically employed in Active
16M.
III. QUANTIZER CONSIDERATIONS FOR PASSIVE 16MS
A. Quantizer Gain Estimation
In order to maximize quantization noise suppression,
we must maximize quantizer gain AQ. To conceptually
understand how to do this, we will first model the quantizer
using a linear approximation, as illustrated in Fig. 1. Several
approaches exist in literature [4], [9]. In this work we will
utilize one previously applied in P16Ms, which uses the
ratio of the RMS levels of the quantizer output and input
signals [9]. We will initially neglect thermal noise, focusing
exclusively on quantization noise. This approach allows the
designer to sufficiently lower the quantization noise level to
achieve a Signal-to-Quantization Noise Ratio (SNQR) greater
than their SNR target [4]. This ensures that when thermal noise
is introduced, the 16M noise floor is dominated by the latter
as is typical for energy-efficient designs [4]. Later in the text
we will introduce thermal noise considerations. The spectral
energy in the quantizer output signal of a typical correctly-
operating 16M with zero input is primarily concentrated
around fs/2. Because the quantizer input signal y equals its
output signal v filtered by the loop filter feedback response

B. Quantizer ADC Noise-Power Trade-Off
As discussed, quantizer (input) thermal noise (en,ADC in
Fig. 1) is a key contributor to the input-referred noise
of P16M, warranting an investigation into its fundamen-
tal noise-power consumption trade-off. In the following,
we assume that both the loop filter feedback and feedfoward
responses HFB( f ) and HFF( f ) have a 0 dB in-band gain for
the P16M, in which case the quantizer ADC’s input-referred
noise and its contribution to modulator input noise are the
same. A situation which is similar for many other conventional
ADCs, such as e.g. SAR, pipeline and flash ADCs. The
transfer function of this noise contribution to the P16M
output equals its Signal Transfer Function (STF), and noise
outside the P16M bandwidth is rejected by the decimation
filter. Because the same argument holds for the input-referred
noise of the first integrator amplifier in Active 16Ms [10],
it serves as our point of comparison. Finally, we ignore
additional power consumption from e.g. clock power in SC
filters for the sake of insight.
Due to the impracticality of multi-bit quantization in
P16M, we only consider the single-bit case, simplifying
the quantizer ADC in Fig. 1 to a comparator. For the
first integrator amplifier in an Active 16M, we make two
simplifying assumptions. First, we take a simple differential
pair to represent its input stage, neglecting noise and power
contributions from its (intermediate and) output stage(s).
And second, we will not consider the noise contributions
of the additional (passive) elements typically surrounding
this amplifier, a detailed discussion of which can be found
in [11]. Finally, because the supply voltage VDD does not

IV. P16M LOOP FILTER TOPOLOGY SELECTION
The relatively low quality factor and large area of on-
chip CMOS inductors at baseband frequencies [15] practically
limits the choice of practical passive filter elements to
Resistors (R), Capacitors (C) and Switched-Capacitors (SC),
thus constraining the set of available filter responses. The
omission of linear amplifiers (and linear transconductors)
means high-Q complex poles cannot be implemented on-
chip, prohibiting the use of high-Q resonator-based loop filter
responses which are typically employed in Active 16M [4].
Further restrictions, on which we elaborate in Section V,
additionally render impractical the implementation of pure
integrator-based loop filter responses. As indicated by (1), our
aim in selecting a filter topology using the available passive
circuit elements should be to maximize its high-frequency
rejection.

A. RC and SC-C Cascade Filters
When cascading RC or Switched-Capacitor-C (SC-C) stages
(as illustrated in Fig. 2a), the frequency-dependent input
impedance of each added stage loads the previous sections.
For the RC filter, this can be understood by noting that the
voltage on CI,1 is influenced not only by the input voltage Vin
through resistor R1, but also by the voltage on CI,2 through
resistor R2, which is a low-pass filtered version of Vin. As a
result, CI,1 experiences frequency-dependent loading. In SC-
C cascade filters, a similar effect occurs where the voltage on
CI,1 is not only influenced (during ϕ2) by the input voltage Vin
(sampled onto CR1,1 at the end of ϕ1), but also (during ϕ1) by
the voltage on CR1,2 (a sample of the voltage on CI,2 from the
end of ϕ2). As a result, the (magnitude) response of these filters
exhibits a shallow initial roll-off beyond their −3 dB frequency
f−3 dB, thus yielding poor out-of-band frequency selectivity as
can be seen in Fig. 2b. Consequently, the use of such filters
in 16M loops yields poor noise-shaping performance [16],
as indicated by (1).
B. The CR-IIR Filter
Because the inter-stage loading effect in SC-C cascade
filters only occurs in ϕ1 between sections, eliminating this
phase would avoid the effect. The fully-passive voltage-
sampling variant of the active Charge-Rotating IIR (CR-IIR)
filter [17] achieves this by utilizing a single switching capacitor
(CR in Fig. 2a), which samples the input voltage Vin at
the end of ϕA. After sampling the input, CR consecutively
charge-shares with integration capacitors CI,1, CI,2 and CI,3.
The (history) charge on CR, carried over from the previous
clock cycle, is dissipated during ϕA. Therefore, the voltage
on CI,1 is only influenced by Vin, and not by the voltages on
CI,2 and CI,3, and the inter-stage loading effect is eliminated.
Additionally, the separate reset phase, implemented in [17] as
required by its active CR-IIR filter mode, is not needed here.
The resulting improved frequency selectivity that this filter
offers, illustrated in Fig. 2a, grants an increased quantizer gain
as per (1) and thereby improved noise-shaping. This makes it a
strong candidate for use in P16Ms, and it is therefore applied
in this work. Finally, we note a further benefit of the CR-IIR
filter: its DT nature renders its transfer function readily flexible
with clock frequency.
V. CR-IIR LOOP FILTER DESIGN FOR P16MS
After establishing the filter architecture around which we
will construct our P16M loop filter, we must determine
its component values. To do this, we begin by determining
the pole location(s) as a function of the P16M bandwidth
BW16M = fclk/(2 · OSR), which we use as a primary design
variable, followed by the noise level of the filter, and give
design equations for both.
Dimensioning the loop filter poles such that its cut-off
frequency f−3 dB is far below the modulator bandwidth
BW16M (as would be the case in a typical active 16M
utilizing active integrators with high DC gain) results in a high
quantizer gain AQ as per (1). However, it also incurs significant
in-band attenuation in HFB( f ), thus increasing demands on the
thermal noise performance of the quantizer ADC. Contrarily,
setting the poles such that f−3 dB ≫ BW16M yields a
HFB( f ) with poor rejection of out-of-band quantization noise
(including at fs/2), resulting in low quantizer gain and thus
poor noise-shaping performance. A practical compromise is to
set f−3 dB = BW16M, as is done in this work.

set by the ratio between the switching- and integrating capac-
itors [17] (CR and CI, respectively, from Fig. 2a). Under the
established filter bandwidth constraint ( f−3 dB = BW16M),
this capacitor ratio is linked to the OSR of the P16M in

VI. CIRCUIT IMPLEMENTATION
This section discusses the circuit-level implementation of
the passive third-order 1 bit 16M implemented in this work.
Although we have utilized GlobalFoundries’ 22 nm FD-SOI
process, our design techniques are also applicable to standard
CMOS technologies. A simplified schematic representation of
the positive half of the proposed differential design, along with
its capacitor values, is shown in Fig. 3a. Its timing diagram is
shown in Fig. 3b. DAC1 forms the primary feedback path,
with DAC2 and DAC3 serving to stabilize the modulator,
as will be discussed later. Equal-size capacitors CR,IN and
CR,DAC sample the input and DAC voltage, respectively.
They combine in parallel from the first-charge-sharing phase
onward, to simultaneously achieve feedback summation and
charge-sharing with integration capacitor CI,1. Afterwards,
they consecutively charge-share with integration capacitors
CI,2 and CI,3, resulting in a loop filter exhibiting a third-order
roll-off. Numerical simulations (without mismatch) indicated
that achieving the performance targets of 80 dB SNDR and
4 MHz bandwidth required a third-order loop filter and OSR
of 256×. The resulting NTF, STF, and loop filter responses
HFF( f ) and HFB( f ) are shown in Fig. 4. Initially assuming
a Maximum Stable Amplitude (MSA) equal to the DAC full-
scale and white quantization noise, the 60 dB in-band NTF
suppression and OSR grant a SNQR of more than 91 dB. Even
with a slightly lower practical MSA, the margin between the
SNQR and SNDR is well over 6 dB. Because the loop filter
and comparator (thermal) noise contribute similarly to overall
P16M input noise, the thermal noise budget was split equally
among them.
A. Loop Filter
The previously mentioned equal-size capacitors CR,DAC and
CR,IN in Fig. 3 together make up the switching capacitor
CR, with their combined value of 560 fF chosen using (8).
An inversion in the 1 bit DAC signal ensures negative
feedback. Four slices each containing one CR,DAC and one
CR,IN are time-interleaved, allowing each individual slice to
utilize a larger portion of Tclk for input signal tracking and
sampling, thereby relaxing the challenge of achieving high-
linearity. Thanks to this time-interleaving, the filter performs
the sampling and all charge-sharing clock subphases during
each Tclk, analogous to the CR-IIR circuit in Fig. 2a. As such,
the output-referred result of (8) still holds for our loop
filter. However, the (approximately) 6 dB attenuation in both
HFF( f ) and HFB( f ) results in an equally increased input-
referred contribution from both the loop filter noise and
the quantizer ADC noise. Additionally, mismatch between
the interleaved switching capacitor slices introduces time-
variance, which frequency-translates quantization noise around
fclk/2 and fclk/4 in-band, resulting in an increased noise floor.
This effect is discussed in Section VI-F.
As can be seen in Fig. 3a, the comparator produces a 1 bit
decision from the loop filter output voltage, at the top-plates of
integration capacitors CI,3, during the time that the latter are
not charge-sharing with CR,DAC and CR,IN. This ensures that
only settled loop filter output voltage samples are processed
by the 16 loop.
To understand the purpose of each DAC in Fig. 3, let us
first consider the transfer function towards the loop filter output
(quantizer input) of each individual DAC. The global feedback
path via DAC1, through its associated switching capacitor
CR,DAC, exhibits a third-order DT frequency response (similar
to HFF( f ) in Fig. 4). Moreover, the DAC1 sample from phase
ϕA only affects the loop filter output voltage in phase ϕD, three
clock cycles later. Together, this results in a large phase lag
near fclk/2, which would destabilize the 16M if not properly
compensated. The local feedback path via DAC3, through
its associated switching capacitor CZ,2, couples directly into
the top-plates of CI,3 within one clock cycle, granting it
a first-order magnitude response and low phase lag. This
is analogous to coupling a switched-capacitor DAC (in DT
16Ms) or a resistor or current-steering DAC (in CT 16Ms)
into the last integrator in Active 16Ms. The DAC1 and
DAC3 paths combine at the top-plates of CI,3, and the ratio
of CZ,2 to CR,DAC sets the frequency at which the response
of HFB( f ) transitions from third- to first-order. However,
because the difference in phase lag between the two paths
is approximately 180◦, a finite anti-resonance occurs around
this transition frequency. The absence of this anti-resonance
in the response of HFF( f ) causes the STF of the 16M to
exhibit a resonance (peaking). To reduce this, the quasi-local

feedback path via DAC2, through its associated switching
capacitor CZ,1, couples into top-plates of CI,2 (analogous
to a DAC coupling into the second-last integrator in Active
16Ms), thus exhibiting a second-order response. As a result,
the combined phase response of DAC1 and DAC2 reduces to
second-order before the transition frequency, thereby damping
the anti-resonance. The result is a Cascade of Integrators with
FeedBack (CIFB) type compensation [4].
Achieving large in-band NTF suppression requires high
quantizer gain, which result from maximizing the high-
frequency rejection of HFB( f ) as indicated by (1). While the
removal of DAC2 and DAC3 would achieve this, the resulting
modulator would be unstable. Thus, there must exist some
(near-)optimal transition frequency, with associated coupling
strength ratio between DAC1 and DAC3, which maximizes
rejection while simultaneously avoiding instability. In active
16M, this coupling ratio can be achieved by applying voltage
gain in the first and second integrators. However, in our
proposed passive 16M, this is instead achieved by the
large ratio between CR,DAC and CZ,2 given in Fig. 3. The
values of CZ,1 and CZ,2 chosen to achieve the desired CIFB
compensation for the 3rd-order CR-IIR filter (shown in Fig. 3)
were found through extensive numerical simulations.
In contrast to our approach, which only uses two small
switching capacitors (CZ,1 and CZ,2) to achieve CIFB
compensation, the 2nd-order P16M in [16] contains an
additional large capacitor, equal in size to the integration
capacitors, which introduces a zero in the loop filter response
for stability. Decreasing the size of this large capacitor would
shift this zero to a lower frequency, reducing the high-
frequency rejection of the loop filter and thereby the (in-band)
quantization noise suppression of the P16M in [16], as per
our (1).
B. 1 bit Quantizer ADC
Because the quantizer is 1 bit, a single comparator is
sufficient to implement its ADC, for which a modified version
of the high-speed dynamic design from [14], shown here
in Fig. 5, is utilized. Capacitive neutralization of the latch
stage using capacitors CNeut., along with a tail charge pump
which increases the overdrive of the input pair (M1 and
M2), enhances its speed over previous dynamic comparator
designs [13]. The negative excursion of the voltage at
the top plate of Ctail is limited to 160 mV. Because the
preamplifier gain is approximately 10×, the (input-referred)
noise contribution of the latch is minimized. As a result of
the charge pump, its input pair MOSTs M1 and M2 operate
in strong inversion to achieve high speed operation. Despite
this, the comparator’s noise-power trade-off is similar to that
given by (5), which was derived for weak inversion, due to its
similar dynamic architecture.

C. 1 bit Quantizer DAC
As the loop filter is switched-capacitor-based, switched-
capacitor DACs with their jitter insensitivity are a natural
choice [4]. As shown schematically in Fig. 3c, they are
implemented as tri-state inverters, which sample a 1 bit voltage
on switching capacitor CR,DAC, CZ,1 or CZ,2. Clock-driven
AND and NAND gates ensure that the DAC inverters are in
their high-Z state (i.e. both n- and pMOST are off) when
their associated switching capacitor (CR,DAC, CZ,1 or CZ,2)
charge-shares with an integration capacitor. This is critical,
as otherwise the latter would be shorted to one of the DAC
inverter supplies, destroying loop filter selectivity.
D. Switch Implementation
Due to the fact that DAC1 couples into the loop filter much
more strongly than DAC2 and DAC3, its output contains a
near-complement of the input signal. Therefore, signal swing
reduces substantially, approximately 20 dB, after CR,DAC and
CR,IN charge-share with CI,1. Further reduction occurs when
DAC2 and DAC3 couple into CI,2 and CI,3, respectively.
This relaxes linearity requirements on later charge-sharing
switches, allowing the second be smaller than the first, and the
third smaller still, reducing clock power. The charge-sharing
switches are implemented as transmission gates.
Contrary to the charge-sharing switches, the input sample
switch must sample the full-scale continuous-time input signal
with sufficiently high (< −80 dB THD) linearity in the allotted
Tclk/2 sample window. Because the on-resistance (Ron) of a
MOST switch is non-linear, significant distortion is produced
unless the sampler settles to a given degree before the input
sample is taken by opening the switch [20]. With increasing
clock frequency, an increasingly wider (i.e. lower Ron) MOST
switch is required to achieve the same degree of settling in the
decreasing sample time window. Unfortunately, this increases
the magnitude of the (non-linear) channel charge injected into
the sampling capacitor when the MOST switch is opened [20].
As a result, there exists a minimum combined distortion power
from both effects for a given MOST switch width for each
distinct clock frequency. Additionally, this minimum distortion
power increases with clock frequency, thereby limiting the
maximum possible speed of operation for a given linearity
demand. However, the minimum distortion power can still
be improved by making both switch Ron and (non-linear)
charge-injection less dependent on the input signal by standard
bootstrap techniques [20] such as the one from [21] applied
in this work. Still, the increasing minimum distortion power
with increasing clock frequency produced by the input sample
switch presented a bottleneck to the maximum speed of
operation of our design.

To understand its operation, let us assume that the divider is
working correctly and only a single output pulse (Q1 . . . Q16)
is high at any given time. We first consider the clock divider
core and its truth table illustrated in Inset 1. of Fig. 8. A NOR
gate receiving a CLK and E(nable) signal produces an output
Q, which equals the inverted CLK signal when Enable is low,
being always low otherwise. This Enable signal is generated
by a NOR latch. By using the previous divider output phase
to set Enable low, and the next divider output phase to set it
high again, only a single CLK pulse is passed to the divider
output Q, as illustrated in the timing diagram.
Although we assumed that only a single output phase Q was
active at any time, this may not be the case if the NOR latches
are not properly initialized. This would allow more than one
divider output phase to be high at a given time, disrupting
proper operation of the P16M loop filter. To force correct
initialization, the reset logic shown in Inset 2. of Fig. 8 was
added to each clock divider core block’s NOR latch. When
RST is high, the reset logic forces the NOR latch in the X unit
block into the enable low state (i.e. invert CLK), regardless
of the state of its two divider phase inputs Q. By the same
principle, but flipping the control logic with respect to unit
block X, all NOR latches in the Y unit blocks are forced into
their enable high state (i.e. force low). When RST is returned
low, the reset logic becomes transparant from its Q inputs to
its 'Q outputs, and clock divider operation continues as if the
reset logic was not present.
F. SNR Degradation Due to Mismatch-Induced Interleaving
Mixing
Capacitor mismatch between the four interleaved switched-
capacitor slices in Fig. 3a, each containing a CR,DAC and
a CR,IN, results in Linear Periodically Time-Variant (LPTV)
behavior with a periodicity of 4 × Tclk in the step response
of the loop filter. As a result, a fraction (related to the
degree of mismatch) of the quantization noise in the DAC
signal around fclk/4 and fclk/2 frequency-translates back
into the modulator bandwidth [22]. Due to the noise-shaping
action of the 16M, there is significantly more noise power
around these frequencies than there is in-band. Thus, even
a small fraction of this noise power frequency-translating
down leads to a significant SN(D)R degradation, resulting
in a high sensitivity to mismatch. The incurred performance
degradations are present in the measurements discussed in the
following section.

VIII. CONCLUSION
In this work, we extended the bandwidth of Passive 16Ms
(P16Ms) towards 4 MHz. Two key challenges unique to
the design of P16Ms not found in their active counterparts
were discussed. These involved the input noise criticality of
the quantizer and poor selectivity of passive on-chip (loop)
filters. It was shown that for the 1 bit quantizer case, the
noise versus power consumption trade-off of its comparator
is identical to that of the first amplifier in Active 16Ms.
Because the absence of amplifiers disallows the use of
traditional 16M loop filter synthesis methods, we discussed
the selection of a suitable passive loop filter architecture. This
lead to the selection of the voltage-sampling Charge-Rotating
IIR (CR-IIR) filter, for which we derived 16M-oriented
design equations. Measurements on the prototype 3rd-order
passive 16M implemented in GlobalFoundries 22 nm FD-
SOI in 0.076 mm2 show that it achieves a peak SNDR of
71.9 dB in a 4 MHz bandwidth while consuming 4.4 mW
from a 0.9 V supply, resulting in a 161.7 dB Schreier FoM.
Its 4 MHz bandwidth is a 40 times improvement over state-
of-the-art Passive 16Ms (P16Ms) in literature, which
demonstrate similar Schreier FoMs. The prototype P16M
continues to operate as low as 0.4 V, where it achieves
a reduced bandwidth of 156.25 kHz and a 62 dB peak
SNDR. This allows for use of (an optimized version of)
our P16M in energy harvesting-powered ICs [7]. Moreover,
large-signal out-of-band measurements show that the prototype
modulator’s in-band spectrum is not severely corrupted
by such unwanted signals even a full decade beyond its
bandwidth. This demonstrates rudimentary suitability for radio
applications.

Jeroen Ponte (Graduate Student Member, IEEE)
was born in Harlingen, The Netherlands. He received
the B.Sc. and M.Sc. (cum laude) degrees in
electrical engineering from the University of Twente,
The Netherlands, in 2018 and 2020, respectively,
where he is currently pursuing the Ph.D. degree
with the Integrated Circuit Design Group. His
research interests include but are not limited
to, passive switched-capacitor filters, analog-to-
digital conversion with a focus on noise-shaping
architectures, and radio frequency circuits.
Harijot Singh Bindra (Member, IEEE) received
the Master of Technology degree in VLSI design
from Indian Institute of Technology (IIT), Delhi,
in 2010, and the Ph.D. degree (cum laude) from
the University of Twente, in 2019. He was a
Scientist with Indian Space Research Organization
(ISRO) from 2008 to 2010 and a Senior Design
Engineer with Cadence Design Systems, India,
from 2012 to 2014. His research interests include
low-energy circuit design techniques, high-speed
serial links, data converters, sensor readout circuitry,
and low-power radio front-ends. He is a Technical Program Committee
Member of European Solid-State Electronics Research Conference (ESSERC).
He was recently awarded the VENI-2023 granted by the Dutch Research
Council (NWO). He was an Associate Editor of IEEE TRANSACTIONS ON
CIRCUITS AND SYSTEMS—I: REGULAR PAPERS from 2021 to 2023. He was
also part of the 4TU Interdisciplinary Plantenna Consortium investigating RF
sensing principles for plant health monitoring.
Bram Nauta (Fellow, IEEE) was born in Hengelo,
The Netherlands. He received the M.Sc. degree
(cum laude) in electrical engineering and the Ph.D.
degree, with a focus on analog CMOS filters for
very high frequencies from the University of Twente,
Enschede, The Netherlands, in 1987 and 1991,
respectively.
In 1991, he joined the Mixed-Signal Circuits and
Systems Department, Philips Research, Eindhoven,
The Netherlands. In 1998, he returned to the
University of Twente as a Full Professor heading the
new IC Design Group. In 2014, he was nominated as a Distinguished Professor
and from 2016 to 2020, he was the Chair of the Electrical Engineering
Department. In 2022, he co-founded “ChipTechTwente,” a local ecosystem
initiative with 50 semiconductor-related companies, partners, and knowledge
institutions. His research interests include analog and radio frequency CMOS
circuits.
Dr. Nauta is a member of the Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts
and Sciences (KNAW). In 2014, he received the “Simon Stevin Meester”
Award (500,000 Euros), the largest national prize in The Netherlands
for achievements in engineering sciences. In 2019, he received the ERC
(European Research Council) Advanced Grant (2.5 Million Euros, personal
grant). He was a co-recipient of the ISSCC 2002 and 2009 “Van Vessem
Outstanding Paper Award” and in 2023, he received the ISSCC Author-
Recognition Award for its first 70 years, as a top ten contributor. In 2023,
he received the inaugural “Dutch Innovation Award” and the NWO Stevin
Prize (1.5 Million Euros), the largest national science prize in The Netherlands
for “exceptional success in knowledge exchange and impact for society.”
He was the 2013 Program Chair of the International Solid-State Circuits
Conference (ISSCC). He served as the Editor-in-Chief for IEEE JOURNAL
OF SOLID-STATE CIRCUITS (JSSC) from 2007 to 2010. Also, he served
as an Associate Editor for IEEE TRANSACTIONS ON CIRCUITS AND
SYSTEMS—II: EXPRESS BRIEFS from 1997 to 1999 and IEEE JOURNAL
OF SOLID-STATE CIRCUITS from 2001 to 2006. He was on the Technical
Program Committee of the Symposium on VLSI Circuits from 2009 to
2013 and served on the steering committee and program committee of
European Solid-State Circuit Conference (ESSCIRC) from 1999 to 2017.
He served on the program committee from 2003 to 2013 and the executive
committee of ISSCC from 2007 to 2015 and since 2022. In 2023, he joined
the program committee of the Advances in Analog Circuit Design Workshop
Series (AACD). He was the President of the IEEE Solid-State Circuits Society
from 2018 to 2019. He served two terms as a Distinguished Lecturer for the
IEEE Solid-State Circuits Society.
