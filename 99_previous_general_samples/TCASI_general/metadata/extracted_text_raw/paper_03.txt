IEEE TRANSACTIONS ON CIRCUITS AND SYSTEMS—I: REGULAR PAPERS, VOL. XX, NO. YY, MONTH YEAR 1
Frequency-Transformation-Based Co-Designed
Lowpass-Single/Multi-Passband-Highpass RF Filters
Roberto Gómez-Garcı́a, Fellow, IEEE, Li Yang, Senior Member, IEEE, Mohamed Malki, Graduate Student
Member, IEEE, and José-Marı́a Muñoz-Ferreras, Member, IEEE
Abstract—The theoretical design and practical development of
RF analog filtering devices with co-integrated lowpass, single/
multi-band bandpass, and highpass transfer functions is reported.
For this purpose, two different classes of generalized frequency
transformation that convert the equivalent normalized lowpass
filter prototype into the desired RF filter with several co-designed
filtering actions are proposed. They realize a frequency mapping
of the reactance of a normalized unitary capacitor into that of
a one-port single/multi-resonance cell with added lowpass and
highpass filtering capabilities. For these devised frequency trans-
formations, the theoretical foundations and various illustrative
filter examples designed at the ideal-circuit-model level are pre-
sented. In addition, higher-selectivity filter architectures based on
the generation of additional out-of-band transmission zeros (TZs)
by means of cross-coupling techniques are shown. Afterwards, the
extension of this design methodology to RF multi-functional fil-
tering components, such as input-reflectionless/absorptive filters
based on complementary-diplexer circuit networks and two-way
filtering power-distribution circuits, is also demonstrated. Fur-
thermore, two design examples of distributed-element and invert-
erless lumped-element RF filters are provided. Besides, for the
distributed-element circuit, a proof-of-concept microstrip proto-
type is manufactured and measured as experimental validation.
Index Terms—Analog filter, absorptive filter, bandpass filter,
co-designed circuit, filtering power divider, frequency trans-
formation, highpass filter, lowpass filter, lumped-element filter,
microstrip filter, microwave filter, multi-functional circuit, planar
filter, reflectionless filter, RF filter, transmission zero (TZ).
I. INTRODUCTION
DURING the last two decades, a considerable research
progress on the development of RF/microwave filters
with multi-band operational characteristics has been con-
ducted. This has been largely motivated by their potential
need for emerging multi-purpose RF-wireless-communications
and remote-sensing applications, in which the exploitation of
several spectral bands at the same time is advantageous [1].
In this manner, a vast plurality of multi-band bandpass filters
for the simultaneous acquisition of multiple desired RF signals
corresponding to different services has been reported [2], [3],
[4], [5], [6], [7], [8]. The same applies to RF multi-band band-
stop or multi-notched-band filters, as a hardware mechanism
that may allow the implementation of the required protection
for RF systems to unwanted jamming/interference RF signals
in highly-congested electromagnetic (EM) environments [9],
[10], [11]. Note that although the existing variety of prior-art

RF multi-band filters in terms of underlying RF operational
principles and circuit structures is very rich, those approaches
using single-to-multi-band frequency transformations deserve
special mention [12], [13], [14], [15], [16]. This is owing to
their intrinsic simplicity, since they mostly involve the replace-
ment of the basic resonators of a pre-designed bandpass filter
counterpart—or of the normalized unitary capacitors of an
equivalent normalized lowpass filter prototype for normalized-
lowpass-to-multi-band frequency transformations—by sets of
resonators to shape the distinct bands. Lately, more-evolved
frequency transformations have been devised, such as those in
[11] and [17] allowing to embed multiple stopbands inside a
wide passband range for the design of RF pre-selection broad-
band filters with co-integrated multi-interference suppression.
Regarding the design of more-advanced RF filters combi-
ning filtering actions of different nature—e.g., lowpass, single/
multi-band bandpass, and highpass ones—in composite trans-
fer functions, much less efforts have been made despite they
may be suitable for some advanced RF receivers to separate
different frequency regions—e.g., lowpass part associated to
a down-converted wide-band signal and other exploitable
frequency bands. In tunable implementations, such compo-
site filters may also be used to realize variable-coupling-less
adaptive multi-bandstop filters for the dynamic suppression
of multiple jamming/inteference RF signals with narrow-to-
ultra-broad-bandwidth characteristics. For example, in [18],
[19], and [20], RF planar filters with merged lowpass and
single/dual-passband functionalities for frequency-static and
switchable circuit realizations are described. All of them are
based on channelized filter banks, which involve high design
complexity in the inter-connection of all channels to properly
match all the transmission bands and large circuit size as
incorporating more bands needs for additional channels. As
an alternative approach, a lowpass-dual-band-bandpass planar
filter unsing metamaterial-inspired cells is proposed in [21].
Nevertheless, it suffers from very-narrow stopband bandwidth
above the second passband. In [22], a class of multi-stub-
loaded-based double-lowpass/bandpass RF planar filters is
presented. Whereas these filters have the merits of abrupt cut-
off slopes between transmission and rejection bands owing
to multi-TZ creation, they are restricted to such specific
transmission-line realizations and to just one passband plus
the lowpass one—as the upper passbands that are measured
are treated as spurious ones, which are not targeted in the
design process. A super-conductor-technology RF filter with
high-selectivity composite-lowpass-bandpass transfer function
is engineered in [23], but using a two-layer configuration
that augments design complexity. Sharp-rejection lowpass-
single-passband RF planar filters based on transversal-signal-

also been exploited to realize RF planar filters with transfer

Hence, it is clear from this description that all these approaches
are constrained by insufficient design generality in terms of
realizable multi-purpose/co-designed filtering action—none
of them use generalized frequency transformations—, circuit
structure, and/or applicable RF technology.
This paper presents different types of generalized/multi-
purpose frequency transformations for the realization of RF fil-
tering devices with composite-lowpass-single/multi-passband-
highpass transfer functions. The conceived frequency transfor-
mations can be understood as more-advanced versions of the
single-to-multi-band ones presented in the past—e.g., those in
[12], [13], [14], [15], and [16]—by also incorporating lowpass-
and highpass-type terms. By means of their application over
an equivalent normalized lowpass filter prototype to readily
obtain the co-designed RF filter, they produce a frequency
mapping of the reactance of the normalized unitary capacitor
into the one of a constituent one-port multi-resonance cell
with co-integrated lowpass and highpass filtering capabili-
ties. In this manner, the coupling circuit topology and pre-
synthesized characteristics of the equivalent normalized low-
pass filter prototype, such as class of filtering response—e.g.,
Butterworth-, Chebyshev-, Bessel-, or elliptic-type—, order,
and minimum in-band power-matching levels, are directly
transferred to all the transmission bands of the converted
RF filter. Furthermore, the usefulness of these generalized
frequency transformations can be extended well beyond basic
RF filters as long as their frequency-selective behavior can be
represented through the referred equivalent normalized circuit
prototype in the lowpass domain; for example, RF multi-
functional filtering components that merge various RF-analog-
signal-processing actions into the same RF circuit device
for size-reduction and operational-performance advantages,
such as filtering power-distribution circuits—e.g., filtering
power dividers and couplers—, filtering phase shifters, filtering
switches, filtering amplifiers, or even filtering antennas [26],
[27], [28], [29]. To the best of the authors’ knowledge, a
theoretical study as the one of this work about multi-purpose
frequency transformations combining lowpass, single/multi-
band bandpass, and highpass filtering terms has not been
reported in the past. This paper counteracts this lack as a
further step for the synthesis of RF filters with co-designed
lowpass, single/multi-passband, and highpass terms with re-
gard to the above-referred works in this research area—i.e.,
[18], [19], [20], [21], [22], [23], [24], and [25]—, which are
limited to specific circuit networks, responses—e.g., lowpass-
single/multi-passband ones—, and/or RF technologies.

II. RF FILTERS WITH CO-DESIGNED LOWPASS,
SINGLE/MULTI-PASSBAND, AND HIGHPASS RESPONSE
This section reports the basic theoretical foundations of
the two conceived classes of composite lowpass-single/multi-
passband-highpass RF filter. Specifically, two families of ge-
neralized frequency transformation for their analytical design
are described, along with illustrative synthesis examples based
on their resulting lumped-element circuit networks. Besides,
techniques to increase their filtering selectivity by means of the
exploitation of additional coupling paths aimed at generating
an increased number of out-of-band TZs are also presented.

4) Additional Considerations: In relation with the three
sub-types of generalized frequency transformations presented
in this section, as well as the one described in Section II-A,
the following additional aspects must be taken into account:
• The selection of the spectral parameters—i.e., center fre-
quencies and bandwidth-scaling factors—in the suggested
generalized frequency transformations must be done in
accordance to their meaning. For example, such parame-
ters must be chosen so that the embedded stopbands are
contained within the lowpass region in the Sub-Type II-B
or inside the highpass one in the Sub-Type II-C. Similarly,
the passband(s) in the Type I must be located within the
stopband region between the lowpass and highpass terms.
Besides, note that similar design-flexibility properties as
those exemplified in Fig. 3 for the frequency transforma-
tion Type I are feasible for the frequency transformations
Sub-Type II-A, Sub-Type II-B—except single/multi-
passband-highpass responses—, and Sub-Type II-C—
except lowpass-single/multi-passband responses.
• Equation (7) also applies to the generalized frequency
transformations Sub-Type II-A, Sub-Type II-B, and Sub-
Type II-C with ΩII-A(f), ΩII-C(f), and ΩII-C(f), respec-
tively, for the analytical derivation of the expressions of
the cut-off frequencies of the transmission bands, which is
a complex mathematical problem except for simple cases.
• Whereas the generalized frequency transformation Sub-
Type II-A inherently introduces lowpass and highpass
filtering actions in the co-designed transfer function, addi-
tional circuit elements can be added to the converted cir-
cuit to eliminate any of them. For example, input/output
lowpass filtering cells may be added to suppress the
highpass range, so that an overall lowpass-single/multi-
passband filtering response is obtained. This technique
is exemplified in Section IV-A by means of the de-
sign of a distributed-element lowpass-dual-band-bandpass
filter, in which transversal-signal-interference lowpass-
type filtering cells are inserted to mitigate the highpass
range—that, on the other hand, gets distorted by the
spurious resonances and frequency-dependent behavior of
the resonators and admittance inverters, respectively, in
such RF transmission-line-based circuit realization.
• For a certain set of specifications that define the targeted
attenuation mask, all the presented types of generalized
frequency transformations allow to theoretically obtain
lowpass-single/multi-passband-highpass filtering respon-
ses with comparable characteristics. Thus, the election of
one or another of them is mostly determined by practical
aspects related with the difficulties involved in realizing
such transfer function in the adopted RF technology. For
example, broad spectral spacings between the multiple
passbands are more feasible in practice with the fre-
quency transformation Type I, whereas narrower inter-
band separations are better suited for the frequency trans-
formations Sub-Type II-A, Sub-Type II-B, and Sub-Type
II-C. This is due to the difficulties involved on producing
very-strong couplings in RF technologies, as usually
required to create wide stopbands—i.e., broad spacings
between passbands—in the frequency transformations
Sub-Type II-A, Sub-Type II-B, and Sub-Type II-C.
• Similar techniques based on the exploitation of additional
coupling paths as those expounded in Section II-A can be
applied to the generalized frequency transformations Sub-
Type II-A, Sub-Type II-B, and Sub-Type II-C for sharper-
rejection co-designed filters with extra out-of-band TZs.

A. Input-Reflectionless Filter
Whereas most of prior-art RF filters exhibit a reflective-type
behavior, the design of reflectionless/absorptive filters is lately
attracting a large research interest. By including lossy elements
in their circuit networks, reflectionless filters dissipate inside
themselves the out-of-band/non-transmitted RF-input-signal
energy instead of reflecting it back to the source. Hence, they
are less harmful than their reflective counterparts for preceding
active stages in a full RF front-end chain, such as mixers of
frequency-conversion stages and amplifiers [30]. Moreover, as
no RF-power reflections are created by these RF filters, the
use of inter-stage isolators or attenuators—which may increase
overall size/volume, DC-power consumption for active-circuit
realizations, or in-band insertion-loss levels—can be avoided.
In the past literature, several methods and circuit topologies
to design reflectionless filters have been reported [31], [32].
One of them consists of the use of complementary networks,
in which a main channel serves as an approximation to the
overall filtering response to be attained, whereas the remaining
ones—one or several—are loaded by resistors at their output
ports and they jointly implement a complementary frequency
response with regard to the one of the main channel. Thus,
the out-of-band RF-signal-power reflections coming from the
main channel are fully dissipated by the loading resistor(s) of
the other channel(s), so that the total filter becomes fully ab-
sorptive at its input terminal. Design techniques for reflection-
less bandpass filters with Butterworth, Chebyshev, and quasi-
elliptic-type responses and multi-passband filters—e.g., split-
type ones—have been detailed [33], [34], [35], [36]. However,
reflectionless filters with co-designed lowpass-single/multi-
passband-highpass transfer functions have not been discussed.
The above-referred limitation is counteracted here by means
of the generalized frequency transformations proposed in this
work. Taking a complementary-diplexer network and the first
class of frequency transformation in Section II-A—i.e., Type

reflectionless filter with lowpass-triple-passband response has

B. Filtering Power Divider
One of the main and first exponents of co-designed RF
filtering components with multiple functionalities are power-
distribution networks with simultaneous filtering capabilities,
such as filtering power dividers and combiners. This is due to
the simplicity of their design, which mostly consists of the co-
integration of the filtering units in the power-divider/combiner
branches. Despite their realization is relatively straightforward
for the case of symmetrical-power-division/combination ac-
tions, some aspects need to be considered in asymmetrical-
power-distribution implementations—e.g., differences between
the frequency-variation profiles of the power transmission res-
ponses from the input port to the different output terminals—
and designs with isolated output accesses—e.g., location of
the isolation resistors—, such as in Wilkinson-type schemes.
As of today, various filtering power dividers with frequency-
static single- and multi-band bandpass responses using di-
fferent design techniques—e.g., co-integration of transversal-
signal-interference cells or coupled-resonator networks in the
power-divider arms—have been reported [37], [38]. The incor-
poration of frequency reconfiguration in such co-designed RF
filtering power dividers has also been addressed as in [39] and
[40], as well as the co-integration of additional operational fea-
tures, such as the previously-referred reflectionless/absorptive
behavior for one or all ports [41], [42]. Nevertheless, the

IV. DESIGN EXAMPLES AND EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
In order to demonstrate the usefulness of the suggested
generalized frequency transformations for the realization of
multi-purpose filters with co-designed lowpass-single/multi-
band-bandpass-highpass responses in specific RF technologies,
two design examples are presented here. They correspond to a
fourth-order lowpass-dual-passband microstrip filter and an in-
verterless third-order lowpass-dual-passband lumped-element
filter for a reference impedance Z0 = 50 Ω.
For the distributed-element RF filter, a proof-of-concept
microstrip prototype has been constructed and measured as
further practical validation. In the experimental development
of this RF filter, a Rogers RO4003C planar substrate with
the following parameters was used: relative dielectric constant
εr = 3.55, dielectric thickness h = 0.813 mm, metal thickness
t = 18 µm, and dielectric loss tangent tan(δD) = 0.0027. The
EM simulations were carried out by means of the commercial
software package Ansys HFSS, whereas the measurements
were taken through an Agilent N5230 vector network analyzer.
The description of the design process and the theoretical
responses of the two designed RF filters, as well as the EM-
simulated and measured results of the manufactured microstrip
prototype associated to the first of them, are provided below.
A. Design I: Distributed-Element Filter
The first validation example consists of an RF filter with
co-integrated fourth-order lowpass and dual-passband filter-
ing actions developed in microstrip technology. Its ideal
transmission-line-based circuit scheme and conceptual opera-
tional principle is detailed in Fig. 11. Owing to design simplic-
ity for this technology, the generalized frequency transforma-
tion Sub-Type II-A was adopted in this example. Specifically,
the steps described below were followed for its design process:
1) Application of the generalized frequency transformation
Sub-Type II-A given in (9) over a pre-designed equivalent
normalized lowpass filter prototype with the intended in-
band ripple level—i.e., 0.25 dB for this design example—
and Z0 impedance scaling. The ripple-level-referred cut-
off frequency of the lowpass transmission range was fixed
as 0.85 GHz, whereas the center frequencies of the first
and second passbands were selected as 1.4 and 2.2 GHz
with absolute bandwidths of 130 and 200 MHz referred to
the ripple level, respectively. Such specifications were ac-
complished after the numerical inspection of (7) with (9)
and the composite filtering response of the converted co-
designed filter, whose lumped-element and admittance-
inversion-constant values were obtained with (10)–(12).
2) Realization of the lumped-element resonators plus their
adjacent admittance inverters in the converted multi-reso-
nant cell as quarter-wavelength open-ended transmission-
line segments and the admittance inverters of the direct
input-output path as quarter-wavelength transmission-line
segments. To this aim, well-known RF/microwave filter
design techniques establishing the referred equivalences
between these lumped-circuit elements and their transmi-
ssion-line-based counterparts were employed [43], [44].
3) Separate design of the input/output sharp-rejection low-
pass-type filtering cells based on the transversal-signal-
interference approach presented in [45] to remove the un-
desired highpass-type transmission range in the previous
circuit part, which gets also distorted by the frequency-
periodic behavior of the transmission-line resonators—
i.e., spurious resonances—and admittance inverters.
4) In-series cascade connection of the designed input/output
lowpass filtering sections to shape the overall RF filter as
shown in Fig. 11 and fine optimization to maximize the
in-band power-matching levels for its lowpass and dual-
passband transmission regions along with the power-re-
jection levels and bandwidth of its upper-stopband range.
5) Dimensioning of the filter layout with its final EM opti-
mization using commercial computer-aided-design tools.

As can be seen, the bandwidth and power-attenuation levels of

The photograph of the constructed microstrip prototype of

Note that if required by the intended application, higher
levels of circuit-size reduction can be obtained through the
meandering or fractal-shape patterning of the transmission-
line segments of the prototype as reported in [47] and [48].
In such case, an additional fine optimization step of the total
filter circuit to counteract the extra spurious couplings that may
be generated owing to the closer physical spacings resulting
among transmission-line segments may be necessary. As can
be seen in Fig. 13, a fairly-close agreement between predicted
and experimental results is attained. Higher minimum in-band
power-insertion-loss levels are visible in the measurements
with regard to the simulations, which is attributed to higher-
than-expected substrate and radiation losses. Note also that,
as design trade-off, such insertion-loss levels are augmented
in the overall filter—i.e., with regard to those inherent to
the in-series cascade connection of the three triple-open-
ended-stub cells—by the extra input/output transversal-signal-
interference lowpass-type filtering units that were incorporated
to further enlarge the upper-stopband bandwidth. The mea-
sured lowpass transmission range has a 3-dB cut-off frequency
of 0.85 GHz and a minimum in-band power-matching level of
12 dB. The measured first and second passbands exhibit center
frequencies of 1.4 and 2.31 GHz, 3-dB absolute bandwidths
equal to 163 and 212 MHz—i.e., 11.6% and 9.2% in relative
terms—, minimum in-band power-insertions-loss levels of
3.9 and 4.1 dB, and in-band power-matching levels higher
than 11.5 and 20.9 dB, respectively. The measured upper-
stopband range extends from 2.58 to 6.79 GHz for a minimum
attenuation level of 26.9 dB. Note also that, due to the substrate
loss, the effect of spurious spikes in the measured upper
stopband when compared to the theoretical response depicted
in Fig. 12 is largely reduced. The measured maximum in-band
group-delay variations for the lowpass-type band and the first
and second passbands are 3.9, 2.9, and 2.5 ns, respectively.
A qualitative comparison of the manufactured lowpass-dual-
passband filter prototype in Fig. 13 with other related prior-art
filters is provided in Table II. As can be seen, the co-designed
filter developed in this work exhibits all the desired features in
terms of TZ generation at both sides of all transmission bands,
extended upper stopband with extra TZ creation, and low
circuit complexity that results in ease of scalability to higher-
order realizations with increased number of passbands. Be-
sides, it is the only one whose design is based on a generalized
frequency transformation—i.e., Sub-Type II-A in this case.
B. Design II: Inverterless Lumped-Element Filter
As the second example, a third-order lowpass-dual-passband
RF filter in lumped-element technology for the VHF/UHF
range has been designed. In this case, the generalized fre-
quency transformation Sub-Type II-B was employed to derive
the converted co-designed filter network. Afterwards, circuit
transformations were applied to this circuit network to remove
the admittance inverters. In this manner, the negative effects
associated to the frequency dependency of the admittance
inverters in a lumped-element realization in terms of distortion
of the overall filtering response are avoided. It also results in an
implementation with minimum number of lumped elements.

V. CONCLUSION
Various types of generalized frequency transformations
for co-designed RF filtering devices have been presented.
They allow the direct realization of arbitrary filtering respon-
ses with composite-lowpass-single/multi-passband/highpass
terms. For these frequency transformations, the resulting co-
designed filtering networks after their application over the
normalized equivalent lowpass filter prototype in terms of
lumped-element models have been detailed. Besides, tech-
