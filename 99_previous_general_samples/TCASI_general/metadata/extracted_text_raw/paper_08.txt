Multilayer Reflectionless RF Bandpass Filters With
Wideband Quasi-Constant Group-Delay Responses
Li Yang, Senior Member, IEEE, Mohamed Malki, Graduate Student Member, IEEE, José-María Muñoz-Ferreras,
Member, IEEE, Xi Zhu, Senior Member, IEEE, and Roberto Gómez-García, Fellow, IEEE

Abstract—A class of reflectionless RF bandpass filters (BPFs)
with wideband quasi-constant in-band group-delay response in
transmission for high-data-rate digital-communication systems is
presented. These BPFs are developed by exploiting multilayer
wideband microstrip-to-microstrip vertical transitions and lossy
multi-section microstrip lines in complementary-diplexer-based
architectures. A two-port-reflectionless wideband BPF with low-
in-band-variation equi-ripple-type group-delay characteristics is
firstly realized by using two resistively-terminated one-section
microstrip lines. To further flatten its passband group delay, its
counterpart based on lossy two-section microstrip lines is then
conceived to attain extremely-flat wideband group-delay pattern.
Subsequently, by means of a modified wideband microstrip
transition with a short-circuit-ended two-section microstrip line
and a resistively-terminated two-section microstrip line, an input-
reflectionless BPF with two close-to-passband transmission zeros
(TZs) and wideband in-band flat group-delay profile is reported.
In order to obtain a higher-order flat-group-delay wideband BPF
response with more TZs, its structure shaped by two in-series-
cascaded units of the previous input-absorptive BPF section with
two TZs is engineered, in which different impedance values for
their constituent short-circuit-ended microstrip lines are utilized.
The operational foundations of all the proposed wideband flat-
group-delay RF BPFs are described in detail. Furthermore, for
practical-validation purposes, three 2-GHz microstrip prototypes
of these reflectionless wideband RF BPFs with quasi-constant
group-delay responses beyond their 3-dB absolute bandwidths
(BWs) are designed, simulated, manufactured, and characterized.
Index Terms—Absorptive filter, bandpass filter (BPF), digital
communications, flat group delay, inter-symbol interference
(ISI), microstrip filter, multi-functional circuit, multilayer filter,
phase distortion, reflectionless filter, RF filter, transmission zero
(TZ), wideband filter.

symbol interference (ISI) may arise at the detection times,
which are sensitive to jitter errors for high-data-rate carriers—
see Appendix A, in which the benefits derived from using flat-
group-delay filters instead of more-typical filters with flatter
transmission band in amplitude—but not flat group delay—are
theoretically illustrated for a binary digital communications
system example. Hence, it is necessary to equip RF BPFs and
filtering circuits with flat and manipulable group-delay
properties for their exploitation in different RF analog-signal-
processing (ASP) scenarios [6], [7], [8], [9], [10], [11], [12],
[13]. Based on transversal filter topologies and lossy dual-
transmission structures, RF circuits with maximally-flat
wideband negative group delay were reported in [8] and [9],
respectively. In [10], wideband flat group-delay circuits using
microstrip coupled lines were discussed. By means of
acoustic-wave lumped-element resonators, BPFs and diplexers
with in-band constant group-delay responses were presented in
[11]. In addition, in order to circumvent the typical drawbacks
of analog-to-digital conversions in ASP hardware, wideband
RF filters with positive-slope linear-type-frequency-dependent
group-delay profile were presented in [12]. In [13], by
employing coupled-resonator circuit networks, RF dispersive-
delay-structure BPFs with arbitrary asymmetric-type group-
delay responses were suggested. However, all the
aforementioned RF BPFs and circuits are designed as
reflective-type RF devices, which means that the out-of-
band/non-transmitted RF-signal power is reflected back at
their input terminals.
In order to absorb the harmful stopband RF-power
reflections inherent to reflective-type filters so as to enhance
the stability/robustness of adjacent RF active-circuit stages,
the development of their absorptive/reflectionless counterparts
is becoming more attractive [14], [15], [16]. Unlike reflective-
type BPFs, the non-transmitted stopband RF-power echoes
can be mostly dissipated in reflectionless BPFs inside
themselves by introducing lossy elements in their circuit
networks, such as surface-mount-device (SMD) resistors [17],
[18], [19]. In this context, reflectionless RF filters with various
group-delay responses have been recently suggested as in [20],
[21], [22], [23], [24], [25], [26], [27], [28], and [29]. In [20], a
low-reflection microstrip BPF with narrow-band flat group-
delay profile was reported. A switchable absorptive quasi-
constant group-delay wideband bandstop filter (BSF) was then
presented in [21]. Moreover, by means of asymmetrical lossy
stubs [22], negative-group-delay admittance inverters [23],
and transversal-circuit-based power dividers [24], [25], BPFs
with wideband flat-group-delay properties along with quasi or
broadband absorptive behavior were engineered, respectively.
Furthermore, input- and two-port-reflectionless RF lossy BSFs
with static single-band, adaptive dual-band, and wideband
negative-group-delay patterns were designed in [26], [27],
[28], and [29], respectively. Besides, by using a two-branch-
channelized circuit, a class of input-reflectionless multi-
functional RF BPF with linear-type-frequency-variant in-band
group-delay profile was presented in [30]. Nevertheless, most
of these reflectionless RF filtering circuits are concentrated on
narrow-band group-delay applications, and only some of them
(e.g., [21], [22], [23], [24], [25], and [28]) are equipped with
wideband group-delay responses. Furthermore, they still suffer
from one or several of the following drawbacks: (i) relatively-
narrow 3-dB BW along with limited reflectionless/absorptive
frequency range, (ii) degraded in-band group-delay flatness, or
(iii) poor filtering selectivity due to the lack of close-to-
passband TZs for BPF applications.
In this paper, following the previous works in [30] and [31]
on developing multilayer two-port-absorptive wideband BPFs
and input-absorptive wideband sharp-rejection BPFs with
remarkable passband amplitude flatness and high selectivity,
respectively, a class of RF wideband BPFs with quasi-constant
group-delay responses along with broadband reflectionless
behavior is proposed. As illustrated in Fig. 1(c) and (d), the
BPFs reported in this work are designed to exhibit wideband
flat-group-delay responses beyond their corresponding 3-dB
absolute-BW ranges when compared to their conceptual ideal
counterparts with fully-flat group delay at the whole frequency
spectrum. The main contributions of this work can be
summarized as follows: (i) realization of two-port-absorptive
wideband BPFs with extremely-flat in-band group-delay
profile, (ii) engineering of input-reflectionless wideband BPFs
with TZs and constant in-band group delay—which may allow
the acquisition of broad-band RF signals with high-data-rate
modulating waveforms while producing reduced ISI levels for
digital communications applications—, and (iii) extrapolation
to higher-order input-reflectionless wideband BPFs with flat
in-band group delay and multiple pairs of TZs. To the best of
the authors’ knowledge, this is the first time to design multi-
functional wideband RF BPFs simultaneously featuring quasi-

The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Based on a
wideband microstrip vertical transition and two shunt
resistively-terminated one-section microstrip lines, two-port-

microstrip lines is then developed. Afterwards, through a
modified microstrip transition with a short-circuit-ended two-

comparison between the developed absorptive wideband flat-

BPFs with quasi-constant in-band group-delay properties is
made. Finally, a brief summary and the most relevant
concluding remarks of this work are provided in Section V.
II. WIDEBAND FLAT-GROUP-DELAY BPFS WITH TWO-PORT
REFLECTIONLESS BEHAVIOR
The generalized transmission-line (TL) equivalent circuit of
the proposed family of wideband flat-group-delay BPFs with
two-port-reflectionless behavior is depicted in Fig. 2. As can
be seen, it consists of two main circuit parts: 1) a microstrip-
to-microstrip vertical transition for attaining a wideband BPF
response [33] and 2) two shunt resistively-terminated multi-
section microstrip lines for absorbing the unwanted stopband
RF-power reflections. The electrical length θ of its constituent
open-circuit-ended stubs, short-circuit-ended stubs, and shunt
in-series-cascaded TL sections is herein set as π/2—i.e.,
quarter-wavelength—at the design frequency f0 = 2 GHz, and
their associated characteristic impedances are Zm, Zs, Zm1, …,
and Zm(i+1). Zin refers to the input impedance of the shunt open-
circuit-ended stub and the resistively-terminated multi-section
microstrip line, Z0 is the 50-Ω port impedance, and R
represents the 50-Ω resistance of the terminating resistors that
are used to attain the intended broadband two-port-
reflectionless behavior. Besides, because the radiation,
impedance-dispersion, and open-/short-circuit-ending effects
may vary the impedance values of the coupled microstrip and
slotline resonators in the practical electromagnetic (EM)
design, three pairs of transformers with turns ratios of Nm, Ns,
and Nm1 are utilized [34]. In the following, the properties of
the devised two-port-reflectionless wideband flat-group-delay
BPFs are determined for various cases of their shunt
resistively-terminated microstrip lines and numbers of their
constituent in-series-cascaded sections.

III. WIDEBAND FLAT-GROUP-DELAY BPFS WITH TZS AND
INPUT-REFLECTIONLESS BEHAVIOR
Based on the design approach of wideband two-port-
absorptive quasi-constant-group-delay BPFs in Section II, the
extrapolation to input-reflectionless wideband flat-group-delay
BPFs with TZs using a shunt resistively-ended two-section
microstrip line is addressed here. Unlike in the two-port-
absorptive BPFs in Section II, the BPF channel in the devised
input-absorptive BPFs is made up of a modified wideband
microstrip transition with a short-circuit-ended two-section
microstrip line loaded at the output port, whereas only one
shunt lossy microstrip line is employed. Its generalized
equivalent circuit using a resistively-terminated multi-section
microstrip line is depicted in Fig. 10. Similarly, the electrical
length of all the TL circuit elements—i.e., open-circuit-ended
stub, short-circuit-ended stubs and two-section TL, and shunt
in-series-cascaded TL sections—is assumed as θ = π/2—i.e.,
quarter-wavelength—at f0 = 2 GHz, and their associated line-
impedance parameters are designated as Zm, Zs, Z1, Z2, Zm1, …,
and Zm(i+1), respectively. The input impedances of the shunt-
connected open-circuit-ended stub and lossy in-series-
cascaded TL sections and the short-circuit-ended two-section
TL are referred to as Zin and Zin1, respectively. Moreover,
compared with the equivalent circuit in Fig. 2 of the two-port-
reflectionless wideband BPFs, an additional transformer with
turns ratio of Nm2 is used to model the impedance variations of
the short-circuit-ended two-section TL at the output port.

In order to investigate adjustment flexibility in terms of 3-
dB-passband BW while preserving the wideband flat-group-
delay properties of the conceived input-absorptive BPF with

obtained maximum in-band group-delay variation is gradually
increased versus a decreased τg(f0) value. On the other hand, as
revealed by Fig. 13, the 3-dB-passband BW and the input-

can be increased by using a resistively-terminated TL section
with more cascaded TLs. It is also observed that the maximum
in-band group-delay variations are further reduced as a higher
number of cascaded TLs is used in the shunt lossy TL section.

V. SUMMARY AND CONCLUDING REMARKS
A class of two-layer reflectionless microstrip RF BPFs with
wideband flat-group-delay responses has been reported. By
means of a microstrip-to-microstrip vertical transition and two
shunt resistively-terminated one-section microstrip lines, the
properties of two-port-reflectionless wideband BPFs with low-
in-band-variation equi-ripple-type group-delay characteristics
have been firstly determined. Then, its wideband extremely-
flat-group-delay BPF counterpart has been conceived by
exploiting shunt resistively-ended two-section microstrip lines.
Besides, by using a modified microstrip vertical transition
with a short-circuit-ended two-section microstrip line at the
output port and a shunt lossy two-section microstrip line, a
type of input-reflectionless wideband BPF with a pair of TZs
and flat-group-delay profile has been designed. By cascading
two replicas of such BPF unit with two TZs, a novel kind of
high-order wideband flat-group-delay BPF with more pairs of
close-to-passband TZs and increased out-of-band-attenuation
levels has been discussed. The RF operational principles and
theoretical foundations of these engineered wideband BPFs
have been presented. Moreover, three experimental proof-of-
concept prototypes of the engineered two-layer absorptive
wideband flat-group-delay BPF devices have been designed,
simulated, constructed, and tested. The fairly-close agreement
obtained between their EM-simulated and measured results
has validated the devised design concept. This family of RF
BPFs, which show wideband quasi-flat group-delay responses
and broadband absorptive behavior, are expected to find
application in modern high-data-rate digital-communication
systems with highly-stringent phase-distortion requisites.
Further research work to be carried out is the development of a
complete high-date-rate digital-communication system and the
practical evaluation of the developed RF BPFs into it for RF
signals employing different digital-modulation schemes (e.g.,
quadrature amplitude or phase-shift keying modulations).
APPENDIX A
To illustrate the benefits referred in Section I derived from
the use of flat-group-delay filters to process communications
signals in scenarios with stringent phase-distortion requisites,
the simulated eye diagrams for a binary communications
system example are shown in Fig. 22. Specifically, three cases
consisting of employing a non-flat-group-delay filter, a raised-
cosine filter, and a flat-group-delay filter are contemplated.
Fig. 22(a) shows a simulated eye diagram corresponding to
the output signal of a conventional filter with a non-flat group-
delay profile—i.e., with remarkable variations or peaks in the
transition bands. As can be seen, there is no sampling instant
for which the ISI phenomenon is circumvented. Conversely,
Fig. 22(b) shows the eye diagram for the output signal of a
raised-cosine filter. In this case, there is an optimum sampling
time—i.e., the center of the eye—for which there is no ISI.
However, small jitter errors would lead to undesired ISI. This
jitter can be more apparent for high data rates as in wideband
digital-communications scenarios, which intrinsically involve
small symbol periods. This referred intolerance to jitter is
easily observed in the zero-crossing intervals of the eye
diagram in Fig. 22(b). Contrary to that, a flat group-delay filter
processing communications signals does not add ISI effects at
any instant in the symbol period, thus being fully tolerant to
possibly arising jitter errors. This is demonstrated in Fig. 22(c),
which attests the benefits of using this type of filter in terms of
ISI despite its rounded-shaped transmission band in amplitude.
