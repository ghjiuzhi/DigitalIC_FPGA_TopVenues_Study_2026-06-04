When Random is Bad: Selective CRPs for
Protecting PUFs against Modeling Attacks
Mieszko Ferens, Edlira Dushku, Sokol Kosta
Abstract—Resource-constrains are a significant challenge when
designing secure IoT devices. To address this problem, the
Physical Unclonable Function (PUF) has been proposed as a
lightweight security primitive capable of hardware fingerprinting.
PUFs can provide device identification capabilities by exploiting
random manufacturing variations, which can be used for authen-
tication with a verifier that identifies a device through challenge-
response interactions with its PUF. However, extensive research
has shown that PUFs are inherently vulnerable to Machine
Learning (ML) modeling attacks. Such attacks use challenge-
response samples to train ML algorithms to learn the underlying
parameters that define the physical PUF. In this paper, we present
a defensive technique to be used by the verifier called Selective
CRPs. We propose generating challenges selectively, instead of
randomly, to negatively affect the parameters of ML models
trained by attackers. Specifically, we provide three methods:
Binary-coded with Padding (BP), Random Shifted Pattern (RSP),
and Binary Shifted Pattern (BSP). We characterize them based
on Hamming Distance patterns, and evaluate their applicability
based on their effect on the uniqueness, uniformity, and reliability
of the underlying PUF implementation. Furthermore, we analyze
and compare their resilience to ML modeling with the traditional
random challenges on the well-studied XOR PUF, Feed-Forward
PUF, and Lightweight Secure PUF, showing improved resilience
of up to 2 times the number of CRPs. Finally, we suggest using
our method on the Interpose PUF to counter reliability-based
attacks which can overcome Selective CRPs and show that up to
4 times the number of CRPs can be exchanged securely.
Index Terms—Physical Unclonable Function, PUF, selective
CRP, modeling attack, hardware security, IoT.
I. INTRODUCTION
THE ubiquitous and pervasive deployment of IoT devices
can be seen in almost any type of industry (e.g., aircrafts,
power plants, healthcare), with current estimates predicting
almost 30 billion devices deployed in 2030 [1]. However,
the wide expansion of IoT devices creates a growing concern
about their security capabilities, as they handle sensitive data
and security-critical systems while possessing a resource-
constrained nature. To provide low-cost and high-security,
several lightweight security solutions have been proposed,
one of which is the Physical Unclonable Function (PUF) [2].
PUFs are physical circuits with low hardware overhead that
are sensitive to random manufacturing variations, providing a
unique and physically unclonable hardware fingerprint.
A PUF generates specific outputs in response to specific
inputs, known as Challenge-Response Pairs (CRPs), which

can be used for different applications [3], [4]. Depending on
the application requirements, two main types of PUFs can be
used: Weak PUFs or Strong PUFs [5], [6]. Weak PUFs have
a limited number of unique inputs (challenges) that can be
applied to them, which makes them an interesting solution for
on-device applications (e.g., tamper-proof storage [4], proof-
of-execution [2], code that only runs on specific devices [2]).
On the other hand, Strong PUFs have a large number of unique
CRPs that increases exponentially with the size of the PUF.
This property makes it infeasible to exhaustively collect all
CRPs and clone the PUF even if they are exposed to an
attacker. Thus, Strong PUFs enable other applications like
device authentication [7], which is the focus of this paper.
Specifically for lightweight IoT devices, implementation of
authentication using Strong PUFs attempts to exclude other
costly hardware security features. As such, it is commonly
assumed that an attacker will be able to obtain a subset
of CRPs of the PUF, either directly from the device or its
communication channel, with active or passive techniques.
This assumption combined with the extensively researched
Machine Learning (ML)-based modeling attacks [8], [9] is a
fundamental impersonation vulnerability of PUFs.
To prevent attacks on PUFs, two main defensive strategies
have emerged. The first strategy involves adding non-linearity
and complexity to the PUF’s model [5] by increasing its size
(i.e., the number of basic components) but without changing
its fundamentals. The second approach is to create a so-
called Controlled PUF [6] by adding additional modules to
the PUF that can support more complex modes of operation
and communication protocols with the verifier. However, both
strategies deviate from the original lightweight proposal of
PUFs by adding more hardware overhead. Moreover, many
attacks have been successfully conducted on the improved
PUF proposals [6], [8], [9].
In this paper, we present a defense mechanism against
passive ML modeling attacks based on Selective CRPs ex-
panding our previous work [16]. Our approach is agnostic
to most Strong delay-based PUFs and does not require any
additional hardware or a Controlled PUF. In particular, we
use selectively chosen challenges by the verifier, instead of
the traditional random ones. Specifically, we use three main
methods [16], each with unique advantages that can adapt to
different use cases: Binary-coded with Padding (BP), Random
Shifted Pattern (RSP), and Binary Shifted Pattern (BSP).
In our previous work [16], we compared these methods with
the traditional random challenges by attacking known PUFs
with well-researched state-of-the-art ML attacks, namely, Lo-
gistic Regression (LR) and Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) [9].

II. RELATED WORK
This section discusses the related works on the topics of: (i)
modeling attacks on Strong PUFs and (ii) defensive techniques
against them. The goal is to select the most relevant attacks
on which our security analysis will be based and identify the
defensive technique trends that motivate our approach. Table I
provides a summary of the most relevant papers.
A. Modeling attacks
Strong PUFs are required for authentication purposes, and
the most prominent type is the Arbiter PUF (APUF) [3].
The first impactful work on ML-based modeling attacks was
presented by Rühmair et al. [11], which was also extended to
FPGA implementations of PUFs [12]. This work uses Sup-
port Vector Machine (SVM), LR, and Evolutionary Strategies
(ES) to successfully attack APUF, FF PUF, XOR PUFs, and
LSPUF. A follow-up to the work by Tobisch et al. [17]
improved the LR attack and showed that it can break the
noise bifurcation countermeasure [18]. In a similar study by
Hospodar et al. [19], SVM and MLP are used to attack APUFs
and 2-XOR PUFs implemented in CMOS technology. A dif-
ferent but very relevant vulnerability of APUF-based designs
is their reliability. This was exploited by Becker [13] using the
Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolutionary Strategies (CMA-
ES) algorithm.
In [20], Alkatheiri et al. proposed an MLP model to
model FF PUFs. Similarly, Aseeri et al. [21] followed by
Mursi et al. [15] create a Neural Network (NN) configuration
that greatly improves the modeling of large XOR PUFs using
the MLP algorithm. Following a different approach, Santikel-
lur et al. [22] employ the Efficient CANDECOMP/PARAFAC
Tensor Regression Network (ECP-TRN) to also break large
XOR PUFs, and even some smaller ones which use a Linear-
Feedback Shift Register (LFSR) or Pseudo-Random Number
Generator (PRNG) to obfuscate their challenge-response map-
ping. In recent years, Wisiol et al. [9] have compared the
performance between the improved LR, MLP, and ECP-TRN
attacks, showing that all have the capability of cloning large
XOR PUFs.
Some variants of the APUF and XOR PUF, namely Multi-
plexer PUF (MPUF) and IPUF, that limit the effectiveness of
previous attacks were also found vulnerable. Firstly, Santikel-
lur et al. [23] show that Deep Feed-Forward NNs are capable
of modeling these PUFs up to a certain size. Shi et al. [24]
use Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) to approximate either
the logical gates or the challenge-response relationship of
MPUFs. Alamro et al. [25] replicate these attacks on MPUF

Discussion. Previous works on modeling attacks mainly
focus on delay-based PUF based on the APUF. Many modeling
attacks are viable, requiring the creation of large and complex
variants, of which the XOR PUF is a prominent benchmark.
Solutions that involve using Controlled PUFs present a way to
reduce overhead, but smaller Controlled PUFs have been found
vulnerable to tailor-made attacks that exploit the increased
attack surface. This motivates us to avoid Controlled PUF
while reducing the size of bare (uncontrolled) PUF, for which
we find that LR and MLP attacks are the most efficient.
B. Defensive techniques
Initially, attempts to make PUFs more secure consisted
of increasing their size and complexity at the cost of more
hardware resources. Starting from the APUF [3], Gassend et al.
introduced non-linearity by adding Feed-Forward loops cre-
ating an FF PUF in [3]. A different approach was seen in
Lim et al. [7], where multiple APUF were put in parallel and
their outputs XORed, creating an XOR PUF.
Instead of increasing the size of PUFs, some authors choose
to build more elaborate designs. Majzoobi et al. [10] proposed
the LSPUF by including input and output networks to the XOR
PUF. Sahoo et al. [28] use multiplexers to select different
independent APUFs creating the MPUF. Nguyen et al. [14]
create a two layered PUF called the IPUF where the upper
layer inserts an additional interpose bit into the challenge
of the lower layer. Wang et al. [29] propose the Dual-Mode
PUF which can unpredictably switch between two modes of
operation. Expanding the same idea, the Configurable Tristate
PUF (CT PUF) [30] by Zhang et al. adds a third mode.
The problem with the previous approaches is that, ulti-
mately, CRPs can be used as training data for ML algo-
rithms. In response, the concept of Controlled PUF was in-
troduced [31]. An example is the PUF with Randomized chal-
lenge (RPUF) by Ye et al. [32] which randomizes the challenge
that stimulates a Strong PUF by means of a PRNG followed
by an LFSR. Another option is the Cyclic Redundancy Check
PUF (CRC-PUF) by Dubrova et al. [33] which randomizes
each input challenge with a CRC generator with a changing
polynomial. Hou et al. [34] use an LFSR to desynchronize
the input challenge from the response bits, creating the Shift
Register PUF (SRPUF). Similarly, the Dynamically Config-
urable Hybrid PUF (DCH PUF) by Wang et al. [35] changes
its state via an LFSR to generate different outputs. Finally, the
response-Feedback-based Lightweight Anti-Machine-learning-
attack (FLAM) PUF was proposed by Wu et al. [36] and also
uses an LFSR to generate responses which look independent
from the input challenges to an attacker.
Based on the use of Controlled PUFs, different authenti-
cation protocols have been proposed. Paral et al. present a
Pattern Matching Key Generation (PMKG) scheme in [37]

secure PUFs have been made by compromising more hardware
resources or creating Controlled PUFs. However, both solu-

PUFs. We identify that the most lightweight security approach
would be improving the security of bare (uncontrolled) PUF.
For this reason, we consider an alternative approach based on
Selective CRPs from our previous work [16], which we extend
in this paper. We also note that, to the best of our knowledge,
random choice of challenges is the only approach used for
bare PUFs and is our sole target for comparison.
III. PROBLEM STATEMENT AND METHODOLOGY
The typical authentication use case using PUFs consists of
a server verifying the identity of a PUF by using prerecorded
CRPs. Acting as a verifier, the server challenges the PUF to
check if its response is the expected one, without reusing CRPs
to prevent replay attacks. Since physically probing a PUF will
likely modify its characteristics and affect the measurements,
the PUF is physically tamper-proof or tamper-evident. Thus, as
long as a software clone cannot be created, the system remains
secure. However, as reported in Section II, many research
works have shown that ML modeling attacks can effectively
create clones of PUFs. For this reason, we propose a different
approach: instead of modifying the PUFs themselves, we only
change the training data that is exposed to the attacker.
Fig. 1 visualizes the intuition behind our idea, which is
based on the observation that ML models are heavily reliant
on training data. Challenge bits for each CRP determine what

V. PROPOSED SELECTIVE CRP SUBSETS
For our proposal, we craft different CRP subsets in which
challenges limit the number of examples where bits are flipped.
This way, many of the delay parameters of the underlying
PUF model, as described in (2), are not exposed. There are
multiple ways to accomplish this obfuscation, and we identify
three main types of possible Selective CRP subsets and give
an example for each. Fig. 3 shows these three examples, with
all actively used bits in each challenge marked.
a) Binary-coded with Padding CRPs (BP): The first
method we propose, called BP, is based on using a binary table
for generating challenges, where the challenges are taken from
the table in order. This introduces new values for previously
unused bits at a rate of 2c
challenges, where c is the most
significant bit in the challenge that has been flipped once.
Since this becomes too slow for large values of c, we only
use 16 out of the 64 bits of the challenge, filling between
them with 0s (as shown in Fig. 3(a)). After that, a circular
shift is applied three times to generate more CRPs and use all
bits, allowing for a soft-reset of the PUF. The main problem
is the low number of generated unique CRPs available to the
server to operate the PUF for its whole lifespan.
b) Random Shifted Pattern CRPs (RSP): For our second
method, called RSP, we propose a Selective CRP subset

guarantee that the server will be provided with enough unique
CRPs, but since we are not using random patterns, we stipulate
that training data quality will be decreased. However, an
attacker with knowledge of the pattern that the server follows
and direct access to the PUF can collect responses beforehand.
VI. EVALUATION OF SELECTIVE CRP SUBSETS
Before evaluating the security improvements that Selective
CRPs have on delay-based Strong PUFs we must first evaluate
and characterize the Selective CRP subsets that were defined
in the previous Section V. We do this with three experiments
and one mathematical analysis, specifically, (i) RSP and BSP
require a fixed value for the pattern length p so we optimize
it, (ii) we look for differences in the patterns created by
measuring some distance metrics like Hamming Distance of all
subsets, (iii) we test the uniqueness, uniformity and reliability
of PUFs using Selective CRP to ensure that they are still
practical, and (iv) we formulate the maximum number of
unique (non-duplicate) CRPs for each method based on the
configuration parameters.

VII. IMPLEMENTATION AND OVERHEAD EVALUATION
Selective CRPs are designed as a lightweight solution
for authentication compatible with resource-constrained IoT
devices that does not modify the traditional implementation
of PUF schemes.
A. Real-time authentication and computational complexity
In a traditional PUF authentication scheme the resource-
constrained prover possesses a PUF and the non-resource-
constrained verifier has knowledge of its secret CRPs typically
obtained through a one-time enrollment during manufactur-
ing [30], [40]. Thus, we focus on the computational complexity
of the modified real-time authentication protocol for Selective
CRPs shown in Fig. 7.
The protocol has four main steps. First, the prover sends its
ID to the verifier. Second, the verifier searches its database
of devices (we can assume an ordered table D) to find the
corresponding ID. Only if the device ID is registered will
the verifier extract N challenges and responses for the PUF
of that specific device. The parameter N determines the size of
the authentication string. Note that the verifier uses a counter
i to avoid reusing challenges and responses from the tables
CID and RID. Third, the prover sequentially generates the
responses to all N challenges and sends them back. Finally, the
verifier tests the HD of the registered and received responses,
typically with a threshold of t = 0.05 or 95% of correct
responses.
From the protocol of Fig. 7 it is clear that the prover
only needs to evaluate its PUF N times, a process which is
challenge independent. Thus, the computational complexity is
linear with O(N). Meanwhile, the verifier must perform some
operations of constant complexity in a loop of size N and a
binary search for ID among M devices. Thus, the computa-
tional complexity is logarithmic-linear with O(N ∗ log(M)).

to the traditional PUF scheme due to the inclusion of the

Memory requirements can be important for Selective CRPs
because unlike in traditional PUF authentication schemes, the
verifier cannot generate challenges randomly in real-time. Note
that the memory requirements only apply to the verifier.
Starting with BP CRPs, we have already calculated the
number of unique CRPs in (10). Removing constants and
considering M devices and n bits per challenge, it is clear
that the complexity is exponential in the size b of the binary
table, linear in the size of the PUF and its challenge n, and
linear in the the number of devices M. Note that we have
to consider that n/b is equal to some constant. Thus, we can
conclude that the memory complexity is O(M ∗ n ∗ 2b
).

and BSP is the same and we can easily obtain it from the

IX. SECURITY EVALUATION ON FEED-FORWARD AND
LIGHTWEIGHT SECURE PUFS
Many research papers have proposed variant designs based
on the well-known Arbiter and XOR PUFs. While Selective
CRPs can affect the ML models of attackers for Arbiter-
based PUFs and are thus agnostic to these designs, certain
variants add logic that modifies the challenges that are feed
to the PUF. In these cases, we cannot guarantee that Selective
CRPs remain effective and agnostic, as their mechanism might
conflict with the PUF’s challenge generation. For the purpose
of testing the effect that Selective CRPs have on PUFs which
modify the challenge, in this section we experiment with two
popular APUF variants, namely, FF PUF [3] and LSPUF [10].
The experimental setup remains the same as in the XOR
PUF experiments, but we note that we only test the improved
MLP attack because LR is not an effective technique for these
PUFs as studied previously [9], [23]. We decided the size
of these PUFs by testing the traditional attack and choosing
parameters that lead to a successful modeling attack when
between 2 ∗ 105
and 3 ∗ 105
CRPs are used. This choice is
done to clearly observe the effect of Selective CRPs on the
same parameters and leads to using 4-FF PUF (4 feed-forward
loops distributed uniformly on the PUF stages) and 2-LSPUF
(2-XOR PUF with the input-output networks of the LSPUF
design). The results can be seen in Fig. 9.
The manipulation of the challenge in both FF PUF and
LSPUF heavily affects the RSP and BSP methods. The result
is that both these methods become worse than traditional
random CRPs. Meanwhile, the BP method remains effective.
For FF PUF, BP CRPs become more effective than in the XOR
PUF case, preventing modeling attacks for nearly all of the
available CRP space. However, in the LSPUF case, while the
last two circular shifts have the expected drops in the modeling
accuracies, the first shift (between 5 ∗ 104
and 9 ∗ 104
) does
not. This observation indicates that BP CRPs could not ensure
a secure soft-reset for LSPUF.
In conclusion, PUF designs that manipulate challenges are
not an adequate use case for Selective CRPs. This is a logical
conclusion, as Selective CRPs are based on specially chosen
challenges, which conflicts with challenge manipulation tech-
niques. However, it is worth noting that BP CRPs can still
provide security in these cases, but they must be tested on each
design. Specifically, we show that FF PUF with BP CRPs is
a highly effective PUF design that can nearly secure the PUF
for its limited BP CRP space.

XI. DISCUSSION
Based on XOR, FF, LS, and IPUF experiments we conclude
that Selective CRPs can enhance the security of bare PUFs
by affecting the attacker ML models. The proposed approach
does not prevent modeling attacks. Instead, it expands the
applicability of bare PUFs giving more options to designs of
resource-constrained systems, by avoiding other vulnerabilities
of Controlled PUFs.
The highlights of our analysis are an almost fully resilient
4-FF PUF implementation with BP CRPs and achieving 3
or 4 times longer secure lifespan for a bare (1,1)-IPUF with
reliability side-channel protection. There are several authen-
tication applications for which our proposal can be used,
with the main limitation being the number of authentications.
Previous research has estimated that a pessimistic number
of unique CRPs required per authentication is 400 [2], [3],
[7]. This number means that depending on the Selective CRP
subset, we can have over 600 authentications (for 4-FF PUF)
or 75−100 authentications (for (1,1)-IPUF) with the side-
channel protection. For example, access cards at limited-time
events are a good use case since the number of needed
authentications is limited. Similarly, RFID-tag-based guest
access cards are also a possibility. An interesting idea is to use
the BP CRP subset to ”reset” such a guest card, avoiding a one-
time use scenario. It is important to mention that increasing
the implementation parameters of the PUFs can increase the
number of authentications at the cost of hardware resources.
Regarding the hardware resource consumption, we compare
the FLAM PUF, the CT PUF, and the IPUF based on the Gate
Equivalent (GE) when considering the 65nm CMOS manufac-
turing technology for ICs. The GE defines the complexity of
digital circuits independent of their manufacturing technology.
Table V shows a comparison with the reported GEs of different
gates in the 65nm CMOS manufacturing technology [33], [36].
We can see that due to the lack of additional components (e.g.,
PRNG and LFSR) the hardware consumption is kept lower.
Finally, it is imperative to discuss the security scenarios to
which our technique applies. Since the security of Selective

While passive attackers are affected by Selective CRPs, active
attackers are not, since they can directly query the PUF with
random challenges. Similarly, PUF schemes considered in
existing state-of-the-art papers tend to ignore the existence
of man-in-the-middle attacks [30], [36]. Both active attackers
on the communication channel and man-in-the-middle attacks
can be prevented by implementing mutual authentication pro-
tocols [38], [39]. The discussion is similar when considering
attackers that have physical access to the PUF-enabled device.
In these cases, active collection of CRPs is the same as on the
communication channel, unless invasive attacks are performed.
Given that PUFs have inherent characteristics which make
them resilient to tampering, previous works have tackled the
problem [43], [44]. Note that such considerations are not a
specific disadvantage of Selective CRPs since they affect all
PUF schemes that have no such protections.
For these reasons, Selective CRPs should only be used in
real applications in combination with some mutual authen-
tication scheme and, if the threat model allows for tempo-
rary physical access, anti-tampering measures. Alternatively,
to avoid additional modules, applications with a controlled
communication channel and replacement of devices in case of
theft or loss (e.g., access cards or RFID-tags) are arguably
a better target. Moreover, the soft-reset of BP CRPs also
provides a solution for some of these threats.
XII. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we propose the use of Selective CRPs
with bare PUFs, specifically Arbiter-based PUFs, when the
Challenge-Response Pairs (CRPs) used for authentication are
not random. For years, these PUFs have been considered
insecure against Machine Learning (ML) modeling attacks,
and newer PUF proposals focus on creating a Controlled PUF.
However, Controlled PUFs introduce a larger attack surface
and go against the lightweight principle of PUFs.
Traditionally, the used challenges for authenticating with the
verifier are selected randomly to prevent attackers from col-
lecting the responses beforehand. Nevertheless, we argue that
random challenges have very low similarity among them and
thus provide a lot of information about the PUF’s underlying
model. By creating Selective CRP subsets, namely, Binary-
coded with Padding (BP), Random Shifted Pattern (RSP), and
Binary Shifted Pattern (BSP), we can increase the similarity
of the training data samples (CRPs) used by attackers to train
an ML model. We show that this leads to lower accuracy
models for the attackers, allowing PUFs to exchange more
CRPs before they can be successfully cloned. By using a (1,1)-
IPUF with Selective CRPs, up to 4 times the number of CRPs
can be exchanged with reliability side-channel protection.
Otherwise, 4-FF PUF with specifically BP double the number
of CRPs, enabling certain application scenarios without the use
of traditionally heavy security primitives or Controlled PUFs.
Given results that show the prevention of successful model-
ing attacks on 4-XOR PUF using Logistic Regression (LR) and
Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) even with one million CRPs,
for our future works, we envision similar methods with better
consistency among multiple PUF instances and plan to develop
an automatic system hosted on the verifier for predicting what
CRPs should be exchanged for each specific PUF.
