A Highly Reliable Dual-Mode RRAM PUF
With Key Concealment Scheme
Jiang Li , Yijun Cui , Member, IEEE, Chongyan Gu , Senior Member, IEEE, Chenghua Wang ,
Weiqiang Liu , Senior Member, IEEE, and Shahar Kvatinsky , Senior Member, IEEE
Abstract—Physical Unclonable Function (PUF) has been widely
used in the Internet of Things (IoT) as a promising hardware
security primitive. In recent years, PUFs based on resistive
random access memory (RRAM) have demonstrated excellent
reliability and integration density. Most previous designs store
PUF keys directly in RRAMs, increasing vulnerability to attacks.
This paper proposes a dual-mode RRAM PUF, named differential
mode and flexible mode, utilizing the difference in switching
capability between RRAMs during parallel SET operations as
the entropy source. The proposed PUF can reliably reproduce
keys between cycles, so a key concealment scheme is used to
protect PUF keys from being continuously exposed, improving the
security of the RRAM PUF. The proposed RRAM PUF exhibits
high reliability over ±10% VDD and a wide temperature range
from -25°C to 125°C through post-processing operations. The
flexible mode can generate a significant number of keys for high-
security applications. Since the PUF keys can be concealed, the
proposed PUF is compatible with in-memory computing. It can
be implemented using the same RRAM array as experimentally
validated using a MAGIC operation, thus reducing the hardware
overhead.
Index Terms—Physical unclonable function (PUF), resistive
random access memory (RRAM), dual-mode, concealable, in-
memory computing.
I. INTRODUCTION
Physical unclonable functions (PUFs) have been considered
as a distinctive and essential component within the realm of
hardware security [1], [2]. PUFs offer a unique advantage
by harnessing the inherent physical variations in hardware
to generate keys and identifiers. This distinctiveness makes
PUFs highly resistant to cloning and counterfeiting, providing
a robust foundation for secure authentication, key generation,
and various cryptographic applications.
Conventional PUFs, e.g., Arbiter PUFs [3], [4], ring os-
cillator (RO) PUFs [5], [6], and SRAM PUFs [7], [8], are
based on complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS)
technology. However, the scalability constraints of CMOS
technology not only hinder the continual miniaturization of
devices but also impose restrictions on reducing the overall
area and power consumption. CMOS-based PUFs are suscepti-
ble to environmental variations and having reliability concerns

[9], [10]. Therefore, due to its high density, low power
consumption, and excellent scalability, the nanoscale non-
volatile memory, resistive random access memory (RRAM),
emerges as a promising alternative [11], [12]. The RRAM has
been widely applied in various fields such as PUF, embedded
systems, data storage, and edge computing [13]. In-memory
computing (IMC) based on RRAMs aims to address the
bottleneck known as the ”memory wall” in the von Neumann
architecture [14]. Although the inherent randomness of RRAM
presents challenges for large-scale commercial applications,
this also makes it well-suited for hardware security applica-
tions. RRAM-based PUF designs are considered as a highly
promising hardware security primitive [15].
The intrinsic stochasticity of various random sources in
RRAM, including state switching delay randomness [16],
probabilistic switching [17], and resistance distribution ran-
domness [18], [19], is leveraged for PUF implementations. For
these PUFs, a prevalent approach is used to store PUF keys
in the resistive state of RRAMs and utilize the high-resistance
state (HRS) and low-resistance state (LRS) to generate the ”0”
and ”1” keys, respectively. Unfortunately, these PUF designs
lack compliance with dynamic key generation characteristics.
This approach exposes the keys consistently, enabling an
attacker to use probing to directly access the key and compro-
mise the security of these PUFs. Additionally, when RRAM
is used for in-place data computation, it is not compatible
with PUFs, which means only one process, either IMC or
PUF, can be carried out. The non-reproducibility of PUF keys
across cycles prevents the implementation of IMC on an array.
Therefore, to reduce hardware overhead, a secure PUF that
does not affect IMC is necessary. Moreover, most RRAM
PUFs store keys through the resistive state of the RRAM,
and the number of keys is linearly related to the size of the
array [16], [17]. This results in a large number of RRAMs and
significant area overhead. To improve the resource utilization
of RRAM, flexible PUFs have been proposed [20], [21]. By
flexibly selecting RRAM paths or combinations, more keys
can be obtained. However, due to the cycle-to-cycle variations
in RRAM, their bit error rate (BER) is relatively high. To
address this, a highly reliable and flexible PUF is needed.
This paper is an extension of our previous work in [22].
Based on the previously proposed differential PUF, a dual-
mode RRAM PUF is introduced, including two modes: the dif-
ferential mode and the flexible mode. This dual-mode RRAM
PUF is concealable, preventing key exposure. Specifically, the
scientific research contributions are as follows:

DESIGN
A. Variations in an RRAM
The TiN/HfO2/Ti/TiN RRAM devices [23] were fabricated
for the measurements. Fig. 1 illustrates the resistive switch-
ing characteristic of an RRAM, including I-V curves over
100 cycles under DC conditions. The RRAM exhibits two
resistive states, HRS and LRS. The state switching occurs
when the applied voltage surpasses the forward or reverse
thresholds, denoted as SET and RESET, respectively. The for-
ward and reverse threshold voltages are approximately 0.65V
and −0.85V. Throughout different cycles, RRAM undergoes
random variations in HRS and LRS resistances, along with
threshold voltages. The resistance is influenced by the mor-
phology and structure of the internal conductive filament. The
formation and rupture of these filaments happen randomly and
independently in each RRAM, leading to significant device-to-
device (D2D) and cycle-to-cycle (C2C) variations in RRAM.

C. Differential Mode Implementation
As seen from Fig. 1, the SET process is abrupt while
the RESET process of the RRAM is gradual. Consequently,
D2D variations lead to unpredictability during parallel SET
operations on two RRAMs in the HRS. One device will
switch to LRS before the other. If the applied voltage is then
turned off, one RRAM is in LRS, while the other remains
in HRS. The specific RRAM switching to LRS is random
across cells, making this behavior a randomness source for
PUF. Fig. 5(a) shows that the differential mode implementation
consists of two parallel RRAMs, jointly connected to a ground
RRAM 𝑅𝑅𝐴𝑀𝐺, initially set to LRS. Fig. 5(b) displays the
application of a voltage pulse sequence to 𝑊𝐿1 and 𝑊𝐿2 for
PUF key generation. The process begins with resetting both
RRAMs to the HRS, followed by a parallel SET operation.
Due to inherent process variations, one RRAM switches to
the LRS first. At this point, the BL node’s voltage increases,
affected by the voltage divider with 𝑅𝑅𝐴𝑀𝐺, preventing the
other RRAM from changing its resistance state. The PUF key
is generated by reading the resistance state of 𝑅𝑅𝐴𝑀1. If
𝑅𝑅𝐴𝑀1 is in LRS, the PUF key is “1”; otherwise, it is “0”.
The state switching ability of RRAMs is susceptible to
temperature variations [26]. Therefore, when the temperature
variations are significant, the switching process of two RRAMs
may flip, causing the BER of the differential mode to increase.
Post-processing operations such as masking or temporal ma-
jority voting (TMV) [27] can be used to reduce the BER,
however, such approaches increase the area and reduce the

column currents.
E. Key Concealment Scheme
Parallel SET operation is reproducible because D2D vari-
ations are more dominant than C2C variations during the
parallel SET process. In successive parallel SET operations,
only the RRAM that previously underwent state switching
will switch to LRS, while the other RRAM consistently
maintains HRS, as demonstrated experimentally in Section III-
A. Leveraging this characteristic, a key concealment scheme
is proposed, as shown in Fig. 9. The proposed RRAM PUF
operates in two states, key state and concealed state. Following
a parallel SET operation, the PUF changes to the key state,
enabling direct reading of the PUF keys. When the PUF
keys are not in use, RESET operations are applied to all
the RRAMs, changing the PUF to the concealed state. All
RRAMs are in a HRS, effectively concealing the PUF keys.
Subsequently, the PUF keys can be easily recovered with
parallel SET operations. In traditional RRAM PUFs [16], [17],
PUF keys are directly stored in the form of RRAM’s resistance
states, continuously exposing the key. If an adversary gains

F. Compatibility with In-memory Computing (IMC)
Due to the concealable nature of the proposed PUF, the
RRAM crossbar array can conceal the PUF key when it is
not required, allowing the array to serve other purposes, such
as IMC. The compatibility design of the proposed PUF with
IMC is shown in Fig. 10. Programming the three RRAMs to
different resistance states and applying different enable signals
allows both PUF and IMC to be implemented in the same
RRAM crossbar array. To enhance reliability, all read-and-
write operations adopt the 𝑉/2 scheme. In the implementation
of a PUF, the WL0 is connected to the ground RRAM (𝑅𝐺),
as shown in Fig. 10(a). By grounding the corresponding 𝑅𝐺
and applying 𝑉𝑆𝐸𝑇 to two selected wordlines, WL1 and WL2,
a parallel SET operation can be performed to implement the
proposed PUF.
In theory, it is possible to implement all in-memory logic
computations, including both stateful and non-stateful logic
families [32]. As an example, Fig. 10(b) shows the imple-
mentation of the memristor-assisted logic (MAGIC) OR gate
[33], and the implementation of other logic follows a similar

A. Functional Verification of the Parallel SET Operation
Since parallel SET operation is a crucial operation in both
modes, experimental validation of the parallel SET operation
was conducted. Fig. 12 shows the experimental results of the
parallel SET operation for a pair of RRAMs. In Fig. 12(a), the
resistive switching characteristics of two RRAM devices are
illustrated. Although their threshold voltages and resistance
values of HRS and LRS are different, the SET process is
abrupt for both devices. Fig. 12(b) shows the state switching
of the two fabricated RRAMs during a parallel SET operation,
with a 5ns interval for each measurement point. Initially, both
𝑅𝑅𝐴𝑀1 and 𝑅𝑅𝐴𝑀2 are in the HRS, with 𝑅𝑅𝐴𝑀1 exhibiting
higher resistance than 𝑅𝑅𝐴𝑀2. Upon applying a parallel SET
operation, 𝑅𝑅𝐴𝑀1 switches to the LRS around 65ns, while
the resistance of 𝑅𝑅𝐴𝑀2 slightly decreases but remains in
the HRS. The discrepancy between the resistances of the two
RRAM devices exceeds 10 times, ensuring a high readout
margin.
The resistance variations of the two fabricated RRAM
devices in the HRS across different cycles are shown in Fig.
13(a). There are significant fluctuations in the resistance of
both RRAM devices in the HRS, with a crossover observed
in their resistances. Fig. 13(b) shows resistive state switching
following a parallel SET operation. Over 100 cycles, 𝑅𝑅𝐴𝑀1
undergoes resistance state switching, whereas 𝑅𝑅𝐴𝑀2 consis-
tently maintains the HRS. Thus, the parallel SET operation
can be reliably reproduced over cycles, and the PUF keys
can be repeatedly generated across cycles, demonstrating the
reliability of the key concealment scheme. However, due to
considerable C2C variations of RRAM, the HRS resistance of
𝑅𝑅𝐴𝑀2 occasionally decreases in certain cycles, leading to a
reduction in the resistance ratio between the two RRAMs.
Moreover, the impact of both the SET threshold voltage and
the pulse voltage of the parallel SET operation on the PUF key
is investigated. In Fig. 14(a), the distribution of SET thresh-
old voltages for two fabricated RRAMs across 50 DC scan
cycles is presented. Notably, 𝑅𝑅𝐴𝑀1 exhibits a marginally
higher SET threshold voltage compared to 𝑅𝑅𝐴𝑀2, with some
overlap observed in their thresholds. By changing the pulse
voltage of the parallel SET operation in 0.02V steps over
the range of 0.8V to 1V, the output of the two RRAMs is
shown in Fig. 14(b). Each voltage was tested for 50 cycles,
with the average resistance of the two RRAMs recorded. The
difference in resistance between the two RRAMs is significant.
Consequently, the parallel SET operation remains stable across
the entire applied voltage range, indicating that this operation
is independent of the pulse voltage used. Additionally, fluctu-
ations in the RRAM threshold voltage do not compromise the
reliability of the parallel SET operation.

and masking are indispensable [27]. TMV𝑥 uses neighboring

highly unstable PUF bits. In this study, we employ TMV3

these 100 PUF cells in the differential mode is reduced to
0. It is worth pointing out that as the number of PUF keys
increases, unstable bits may persist. This challenge can be
addressed through more sophisticated voting mechanisms and
higher masking rates.

cal attacks from directly probing the key. Furthermore, ma-
chine learning (ML) attacks have shown security threat to
PUFs. When the proposed PUF is used for a local key
generation, it should be resistant to ML attacks. The key
generation process does not rely on external inputs or outputs
and is not exposed to attackers for modelling. When the PUF
is used for authentication, it requires external challenges to
generate responses. If an attacker collects a sufficient number
of challenge-response pairs (CRPs), it may be possible to
successfully model the PUF using ML techniques. To resist
ML attacks, generic authentication protocols can be applied
[36], [37]. For example, [36] utilizes an active deception
technique to fool/poison the ML attack and thus achieve high
resistance to ML attacks. With such authentication protocols,
the proposed PUF can reach ML attacks’ resistance.

However, due to RRAM’s C2C variations, these PUFs cannot
effectively reproduce the PUF key between cycles. To improve
reliability, they store the key directly as RRAM resistance
states, contradicting the PUFs’ property of generating the dy-
namic key and increasing the risk of being attacked. Attackers
can read the PUF key directly through probing attacks. In
contrast, the proposed dual-mode PUF can reliably reproduce
the key between cycles and thus can conceal the key to avoid
the risk of being read directly, which improves security.
Meanwhile, the proposed dual-mode PUF are compatible
with IMC due to the concealable feature. The PUF can be
implemented on the same RRAM array as IMC, thus reducing
hardware overhead. Due to their implementation on the same
array, they cannot operate simultaneously. In practice, device
authentication is usually performed first and IMC can be
performed only after successful authentication [38]. Therefore,
PUF and IMC can be time-division multiplexed. In contrast,
other RRAM PUFs cannot reproduce the key between cycles,
and store the key in the form of the resistive state of the
RRAM after using the RRAM array to generate the key. After
that, no further operations can be performed on the RRAMs.
Therefore, these PUFs are not compatible with IMC. The
differential mode can be easily implemented with high density,
suitable for lightweight IoT applications, while the flexible
mode can generate a significant number of keys, showing good
potential for high-security level applications.
IV. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we proposed a dual-mode RRAM PUF,
including differential mode and flexible mode. Based on the
parellel SET operation, the randomness of the resistance state
switching capability of RRAM pairs is used to implement the
proposed PUF. The generated PUF keys can be consistently
reproduced across cycles, so a key concealment scheme is
proposed to enable the PUF to conceal or recover PUF keys
as needed. This concealable ability improves the security of
the proposed PUF compared to previous RRAM PUF designs.
Through post-processing techniques, the proposed dual-mode
PUF have a good reliability across a wide range of ±10%
VDD variations and a temperature range from -25◦C to 125◦C.
Furthermore, experimental results demonstrate the excellent
compatibility of the proposed PUF with IMC. In summary,
the proposed concealable dual-mode PUF provide a more
secure and efficient solution for generating keys in security
applications.
