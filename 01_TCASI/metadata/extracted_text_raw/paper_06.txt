A Low-Latency Power Series Approximate
Computing and Architecture for Co-Calculation of
Division and Square Root
Dian Tian1,2, Ningmei Yu1,∗, Minghui Xie1, Jiahao Tang1, Zhuang Feng1, Álvaro Hernández2 Senior Member,
IEEE, Jesús Ureña2 Senior Member, IEEE
Abstract—The calculation of division and square root is widely
used in edge computing related to image processing, clustering,
recognition, and reconstruction, among others. Their common
multi-stage serial calculation takes longer, and requires a higher
latency, and more redundant hardware resources for multi-
dimensional parallel computations. This work proposes a low-
latency power series approximate digital computing (PSADIC)
paradigm and architecture, which achieves a fast low-latency cal-
culation of multi-dimensional mathematical expressions, focusing
on the co-calculation of division and square root. This approach
allows to compute not only the division and the square root, but
also the inverse and the inverse square root. Moreover, serial
and pipelined architectures have been designed here to achieve
smaller areas and lower latencies, respectively. Compared to
the multi-stage calculation with CORDIC (COordinate Rotation
DIgital Computer), the mean relative error distance (MRED)
is reduced by 67% in PSADIC. Under TSMC (Taiwan Semi-
conductor Manufacturing Company) 40nm CMOS technology,
the proposed serial and pipelined architectures achieve a 66.67%
overall latency reduction compared to the CORDIC when using
both its own maximum clock frequency, and a 75% cycle latency
reduction. Meanwhile, both the ADP (Area Delay Product) and
PDP (Power Delay Product) are also optimized.
Index Terms—Edge computing, image processing, power series
approximate digital computing, calculation of division and square
root.
I. INTRODUCTION
NOWADAYS centralized computing puts more pressure on
cloud servers, as well as more cost and carbon emissions,
whereas edge computing at local or remote devices, such
as cloud-edge-terminal architecture, has recently become a
research trending field. In this context, the computation of
digital signals for artificial intelligence, image processing or
communications, among others [1]–[5], is gradually shifting
to an edge approach, as in IIoT (Industrial Internet of Things)
[6], IoMT (Internet of Medical Things) [1], [7], or AIoT
(Artificial Intelligence of Things) [2]. In all these domains
of application, randomly distributed data often require nor-
malization or standardization to enhance the robustness of the

or a combination of those, are frequently used, thus extremely
consuming resources at edge-computing devices. Hence, it
becomes necessary and meaningful to research simple and
fast computation methods for the aforementioned operations:
division, square root, and inverse square root.
For that purpose, there are five widely spread methods
applied for division [8]: digit recurrence, functional iteration
[9], high-radix [10], [11], variable latency, and look-up tables
(LUT). All of them should consider a suitable tradeoff between
the computational accuracy at the remote devices and the
resource consumption. Fitting a curve or a surface is a suitable
way to achieve this balance in hardware implementations.
In [12], the quotient is modeled as a surface composed of
multiple small rectangular planes, then requiring only adders
and LUT. The maximum absolute error percentage (MAEP)
ranges from 0.44% to 0.11%. In [13] the Mitchell-based
logarithmic division computation method is optimized, which
is improved 45.4% and 34.8% in terms of computational speed
and area delay product (ADP), respectively. Also, in [14] a
first pipelined approximate multiplier and divider architecture
is proposed, which efficiently utilizes 6-input look-up tables
(6-LUTs) and fast carry chains to implement Mitchell’s ap-
proximate algorithms, customized for FPGAs.
Furthermore, stochastic computing (SC), approximate com-
puting, and CORDIC (COordinate Rotation DIgital Computer)
systems are addressed for fault-tolerant computing. SC is
a probability-based computing paradigm that provides lower
area and power consumption. The SC-based division and
square root achieve 2.4× and 30× energy efficiency gains at 8-
bit (256-bit input stream) and 4-bit (16-bit input stream) fixed-
point calculation, respectively [15]. However, beyond 8 bits,
the energy efficiency of the SC circuit is lower than that from
the binary circuit, although the speed is actually lower, since
it is proportional to the bitstream length [16]. Interestingly,
in [17] a type of evolutionary algorithm, cartesian genetic
programming (CGP), is used to develop a library of square
root circuits ranging from 2-bits to 8-bits.
Approximate computing is considered a promising approach
in fault-tolerant computation. The technique allows substan-
tially improved energy efficiency by relaxing the need for
full precision [18]–[21]. In [20] a logarithmic approximate
divider is proposed that computes with adders, subtractors,

It is obvious that approximate division computation provides
a large advantage for edge computing. As for nonlinear
functions such as SQRT, sine or cosine, CORDIC systems
with radix-4, radix-8 and hybrid radix, can be adopted [27]–
[39]. In [6] an RDP-CORDIC algorithm is proposed for the
real-time operation of edge computing devices in smart grids.
It employs a three-stage and multi-stage combined iterative
structure to achieve fast computation and low resource con-
sumption for nonlinear functions, by predicting all the micro-
rotational directions. Mopuri and Acharryya [34] used two
circular CORDICs to compute the square root of complex
numbers, avoiding hyperbolic CORDICs and reducing the
area. CORDIC systems have also been used to compute the
Nth root of an arbitrary number but with a long delay [27]–
[29]. In [35] normalized preprocessing and parabolic synthesis
methods were involved to reduce the delay. Mahdavi and
Timarchi [33], [36] optimized the CORDIC systems by using
appropriate data gating techniques and BSD (Binary Signed
Digit) coding.

II. POWER SERIES APPROXIMATE DIGITAL COMPUTING
PRINCIPLES
The main idea of the PSADIC method is to approximate the
denominator of the division to 1 by multiplying by a power
series. Then, the quotient is approximated as the product of
the power series and the original numerator. Based on this
principle, the PSADIC-c paradigm emerges when multiplying
by a particular set of coefficients ci. Additionally, by trans-
forming the denominator into a square root, the PSADIC-cs
paradigm can also be defined. Both, PSADIC-c and PSADIC-
cs, are described hereinafter, summarizing their expressions
and evaluating the resulting errors.

III. PROPOSED ARCHITECTURE AND HARDWARE
IMPLEMENTATION
Section II has provided a detailed description of the pro-
posed PSADIC paradigms, where it is possible to verify
that all the calculations involved may be easily implemented
with shifters, adders, and multiplexers. Furthermore, since
the PSADIC-cs paradigm actually includes all the operations
necessary for the PSADIC-c as well, a single serial architec-
ture for both PSADIC approaches (denoted dual-mode) can

and control signal generation blocks (CS). On one hand, the
iteration blocks can be divided into four sub-blocks, which are
the flag signal generation block (red region), and the iteration

enable the output. It is worth noting that the MSB is the most
significant bit, CPL is the complement circuit, and Eq is the
equality comparator.
In the flag signal generation block, the value ci is deter-
mined by the flag signals s1, s2, and s3. When s3 or e1 are
valid, the iteration ends, and the output is enabled. The blocks
A1 and A2 are three-input adders consisting of a 3-2 CSA and
a full adder. The difference between them is that a multiplexer
in the block A1 is used to switch modes. The full-adder delay
is Tadd, and the 3-2 CSA delay is T3/2−CSA. Then, the delay
for block A1 is Tadd + T3/2−CSA + T2−mux, whereas for A2
it is Tadd + T3/2−CSA.
The critical path in the flag signal generation block is
marked in red; note that all the calculations in the iterative
architecture depend on this flag signal generation block. There-
fore, the critical path in the z iteration is the sum of the red
and yellow zones, in the p iteration it is the sum of the red
and green zones, and in the x iteration it is the sum of the
red and blue ones. The CPL and the XOR gate delays are
TCP L and Txor, respectively, so the final critical path delay
is 2 · Tadd + 2 · T3/2−CSA + 8 · T2−mux + 2 · TCP L + Txor.
As for the sign of f, the signal values for α and z0 can be
obtained separately using an XOR gate in Fig. 4(a), due to
the independence of the x iteration block. The first result is
then calculated with the MSB from the variable xi to obtain
the sign of the quotient f. On the other hand, in PSADIC-cs,
z0 is unsigned, so only the signed α is entered. As shown in
Fig. 4(b), the signed α and xi are calculated with XOR gate.
Moreover, the computational logic and the proposed archi-
tecture are adapted to reduce the path delay, as shown in
Fig. 3(b). Firstly, the mux for control s1 is moved backwards in
the datapath, so that the critical path is reduced by one mux de-
lay. Besides, the two sign selectors from the variable tmpp are
pruned to one. However, in the z iteration block, the mux for
control s1 is increased to two. Secondly, the CPL is a reverse
and adds an operation with a delay of a carry-in. Thus, only the
inverse is retained to reduce the path delay, and the value 1 is
added at the later full adder. Finally, the critical path delay can
be defined as 2·Tadd +2·T3/2−CSA +7·T2−mux +Tinv +Txor.

C. Word Length Analysis
Whereas common two’s complement divisions or square
roots only require one or two operands, respectively, the co-
calculation of division and square root in PSADIC actually
implies three operands x, z, and α, so that the output word
length in this case is not simply two times the input one. As
an example for the integer representation, the bit widths of the
inputs x, z, and α are set to Lx, Lz, and Lα, respectively, and
the output f is set to Lf . In PSADIC-c, since Lα < Lz + 1,
it is possible to state Lf = Lx − Lz + Lα < Lx + 1.
Similarly, in PSADIC-cs, since Lα < Lz/2 + 1, then it is
met Lf = Lx − Lz/2 + Lα < Lx + 1. Therefore, the word
length Lf of the PSADIC output f is the same as the width
Lx. The floating-point representation complies with the IEEE
754 standard, so the output width Lf remains the same as the
input one.
On the other hand, the input requires a certain number of
bits to be appended, and then preserve accuracy, due to the
shifting involved in the iteration process. Fig. 6(a) shows the
word length in the case of an integer precision with L bits

for accuracy preservation. Fig. 6(b) shows the fixed-point case,

VI. CONCLUSIONS
This work has presented a low-latency power series approx-
imate digital computing (PSADIC), including two paradigms,
PSADIC-c and PSADIC-cs, in order to co-calculate the di-
vision and the square root. This method avoids a multi-
stage computation and implements the co-calculation with a
one-stage iteration. The division, square root, inverse, and
inverse square root can also be computed. The M-dimensional
computation was derived to minimize hardware redundancy
in parallel. The feasibility, accuracy, and robustness of the
PSADIC have been verified through Monte Carlo simulations.
The radix-4 serial and pipelined architectures have been de-
signed for smaller areas and lower latencies. Compared with
a three-stage CORDIC, the MRED of the proposed PSADIC
approach is reduced by five orders of magnitude, and the
required number of iterations is reduced by 2/3. In the 4-bit
appended architecture, the iterations of PSADIC are further
reduced by 83%, and the MRED is improved by 75%.
Under TSMC 40nm CMOS technology, both architectures
achieve a 75% cycle latency reduction compared to CORDIC,
and the ADP and PDP are also reduced by 28.78% and 26.97%
for the serial architecture. When using its own maximum
clock frequency for CORDIC, both architectures for PSADIC
achieve a 66.67% overall latency reduction, and the coefficient
is additionally calculated with smaller ADP and PDP for the
pipeline one. Two 16-dimension PSADIC architectures have
been designed for applying the Z-score standardization, and
they have achieved a 75% overall latency reduction and 3.25
times higher hardware efficiency. Meanwhile, the ADP and
the PDP are still more optimized than those from CORDIC,
even saving a divider in PSADIC.
In general terms, the proposed PSADIC presents relevant
flexibility and advantages, thus dealing with some short-
comings of CORDIC in partial co-calculation. In particular,
the proposed paradigm present advantages for applications
that include normalization, standardization, L2 norm, complex
number normalization and others, such as holographic imag-
ing, pulse modulation, clustering, or path optimization.
