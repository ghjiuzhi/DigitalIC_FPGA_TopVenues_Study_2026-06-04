Future Design Direction for SRAM Data Array:
Hierarchical Subarray with Active Interconnect
Hsiao-Hsuan Liu, Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Carlo Gilardi, Shairfe M. Salahuddin, Zhenlin Pei, Pieter
Schuddinck, Yang Xiang, Pieter Weckx, Geert Hellings, Senior Member, IEEE, Marie Garcia Bardon, Julien
Ryckaert, Chenyun Pan, Senior Member, IEEE, Subhasish Mitra, Fellow, IEEE, Francky Catthoor, Fellow, IEEE
Abstract—In sub 10 nm nodes, the growing dominance of
interconnects in chips poses challenges in designing large-size
static random-access memory (SRAM) subarrays. The main issue
is the write failure problem arising from the increased resistance
and capacitance for bitline (BL) and wordline (WL). To tackle this
issue, the SRAM subarray design incorporates conventional
(Conv.) divided WL and divided BL techniques based on 14-Å-
compatible (A14) nanosheet (NS) technology. This approach
allows for various subarray sizes with successful write operations,
resulting in improved subarray-level performance and power
(PP). However, the additional logic gates come with an area
penalty that may degrade the overall performance, power, and
area (PPA) at the macro level due to increased inter-subarray
interconnect overhead. To overcome this limitation, the active
interconnect (AIC) design is proposed with the features of
fabricating another or multiple active regions at the back-end of
line (BEOL) layers. By moving these extra logic gates from front-
end of line to BEOL in the AIC divided subarray design, the area
penalty is significantly mitigated without compromising PP
compared to the standard (Std.) and Conv. divided counterparts.
To achieve this concept, carbon nanotube gate-all-around
transistor is explored as potential BEOL-compatible device. In
this research, a comprehensive design-technology co-optimization
analysis is conducted to verify the value and potential benefits of
up to 65% macro-level energy-delay-area product improvement
by AIC divided subarray design compared to the Std. subarray
design.
Index Terms—SRAM, Nanosheet, DTCO, active interconnect
(AIC), Carbon Nanotube FET (CNFET).
I. INTRODUCTION
In interconnect-dominant nodes, the static random-access
memory (SRAM) subarray area becomes a critical concern due
to its impact on inter-subarray interconnect overhead. This
inter-subarray interconnect, in turn, exerts significant influence
over the overall macro-level performance, power, and area
(PPA) [1, 2]. Fig. 1 presents an insightful graph of the energy-
delay-area product (EDAP) for a 16 MB data array consisting
of subarrays with varying number of row (-R) and column (-C)
based on high-density (HD) 6-transistors (6T) SRAM bitcell.
As depicted in Fig. 1, a significant 56% reduction in macro-
level EDAP is achieved when transitioning from a small- to a
large-size standard (Std.) subarray design. Notably, these
macro-level results have not yet considered the potential write
failure risk (WFR) [3] within large Std. subarray. This EDAP
reduction is primarily attributed to the decreased inter-subarray
interconnect congestion [1]. Remarkably, given the dominance
of inter-subarray interconnects, the overall EDAP continues to
decrease even as subarray-level power and performance (PP)
increases with bigger subarray size [1]. This observation
propels cache design toward larger SRAM subarrays to attain
further macro-level PPA improvements.

A. Problem Statement
However, enabling a large-size SRAM subarray poses
challenges due to the significant parasitic resistance and
capacitance (RC) overhead on both bitline (BL) and wordline
(WL) [1-3]. This increased RC may cause write failures (Fig. 2)
in the Std. subarray design (Fig. 3(a) and Table I) [3]. Note that
the write operation is considered to be at risk of failure when
the write margin (WM) falls below threshold voltage (VT),
which is 300 mV in this work [3]. The scope of system-level
optimization becomes limited to accommodate a wide variety
of subarray sizes for diverse applications and cache
configurations. Consequently, the design technology co-
optimization (DTCO) lacks a complete overview of subarrays’
PPA in advanced technology nodes.

To conduct a comprehensive DTCO analysis involving
various subarray options, multiple sizes of subarrays are
achieved based on the conventional (Conv.) divided wordline
(DWL) [4] and divided bitline (DBL) [5] techniques (Fig. 3(b),
Table I, and Fig. 4). For simplicity, the terms “standard (Std.)”
and “divided” designs are used to refer to the SRAM subarray
with and without DWL and DBL techniques, respectively, in
this work. By implementing selecting and gating circuits to
partition the SRAM bitcell array into several local blocks (LBs),
the effective RC for BL and WL is decreased. As a result, the
Conv. divided subarray design offers significant subarray-level
improvements in PP and reduces WFR. However, this

B. Proposed Contribution
To address this subarray area penalty and maintain similar
subarray-level PP enhancements, the active interconnect (AIC)
technique is proposed for implementation within the divided
design (Fig. 3(c) and Table I). The AIC approach shares
similarities with the concept of 3-dimensional (3D) sequential
non-bonding stacking architecture, which involves a blanket
active layer transfer onto prefabricated bottom devices [6, 7, 8,
9, 10, 11, 12, 13]. Note that it is sometimes referred to as
monolithic 3D (M3D) integration [11] where multiple layers
are fabricated sequentially, which is the opposite of parallel 3D
technology based on wafer bonding [6]. 3D stacking
technology has emerged as a promising alternative to extend
Moore’s law scaling [6], offering increased device density per
3
chip area and reduced interconnect length. Among 3D
techniques, through-silicon-via-based 3D (TSV-3D) stacking is
a notable example of parallel 3D technology. However, it faces
challenges such as low alignment precision, limited TSV
dimension scaling, and taller TSV requirement in vertical
direction, resulting in an increase in overall volume [6].
Consequently, 3D sequential (or M3D) technology has gained
popularity due to its elimination of alignment issues and the
incorporation of fine-grained monolithic inter-via (MIV)
connections [6]. To prevent bottom-level device degradation,
maintaining low thermal budget of the top-level device and
layer transfer processing is crucial, typically below 500˚C [7, 8,
9, 10, 11, 12, 13]. The term “BEOL-compatible devices”
specifically denotes the top-level device in this context. The
key distinction of the AIC technology, compared to existing 3D
sequential technology, lies in the insertion of single or multiple
layers of active devices within the BEOL, which remains a part
of the bottom SRAM subarray. These BEOL devices contribute
to the overall SRAM functionality instead of operating as
separate logic or memory blocks. Therefore, we use the term
AIC rather than 3D sequential to emphasize this unique
conceptual framework.
The proposed AIC technology tackles the subarray area
penalty issue in the Conv. divided subarray design by
integrating BEOL-compatible devices. Several 1- or 2-
dimensional (1- or 2D) options, such as carbon-nanotube (CNT,
cylinders) [14, 15, 16, 17], MoS2 [18], and WS2 [19] -based
devices, could be potential candidates. Concerns regarding 2D-
based FET [20] arise from higher contact resistance and lower
mobility compared to Si FET, which may potentially lead to PP
degradation in AIC divided subarray design. Among these
devices, Carbon Nanotube FETs (CNFETs) have been
projected to achieve large benefits compared to Si FETs [14]
due to their superior electrostatic control and large injection
velocity [7, 14, 15, 16, 17]. Moreover, CNFETs have already
been integrated in the BEOL within multiple commercial
silicon fabrication facilities at mature nodes (e.g., 90 nm node)
[21, 22], leveraging low-temperature fabrication processes (i.e.,
<400˚C). For these reasons, this work considers gate-all-around
CNFETs with high-density CNTs [14] as BEOL devices. By
incorporating the feature of fabricating an active region within
the SRAM subarray’s BEOL layer, the extra logic gates can be
relocated from FEOL to BEOL. Hence, the AIC divided
subarray design is predicted to reduce the subarray area
overhead from up to 30% to less than 5% (Fig. 5) without any
significant degradation in subarray-level PP compared to the
Conv. counterparts.
Multiple nanosheet (NS) FET SRAM subarray options are
thoroughly optimized across three design types (Std., Conv.
divided, and AIC divided designs) with distinct subarray sizes
and numbers of local blocks (LBs) in 14-Å-compatible
technology (A14) [23]. Furthermore, to validate the value of
AIC divided subarray design and motivate more concrete
process development, corresponding DTCO PPA analysis [3]
is carried out at both subarray and macro levels.
II. SRAM SUBARRAY DESIGNS
This study presents the HD 6T SRAM bitcell array and
periphery designs based on A14 NS [23] and A14 CNT [14]
technology for devices at FEOL and BEOL, respectively. A
quick overview of the three different SRAM subarray designs
(Fig. 3 and Table I): Std., Conv. divided, and AIC divided
designs, are described in this section. Fig. 6 and Fig. 7 illustrate
the schematic views of the single LB layout in conv. and AIC
divided designs, respectively, according to the circuit diagram
shown in Fig. 4. Their physical parameters for each signal line
and the corresponding estimated RC per cell (without device
capacitance) extracted by the QuickCap tool [24] are listed in
Table II and Table III.
A. Std. design (Fig. 3(a))
This design consists of peripheral logic, including
address/timing control, precharge circuits, row decoder,
column multiplexer, sense amplifiers, and write drivers, and
SRAM bitcell array. All the devices are placed at FEOL. More
details of the SRAM subarray architecture can be found in [3].
Expanding the subarray size in the Std. design causes dramatic
degradation in RC for BL and WL, resulting in slower speed
and write failure. Hence, the available subarray design options
become limited.
B. Conv. divided design (Fig. 3(b))
To reduce the effective RC for BL and WL for avoiding
write failure, the SRAM bitcell array is divided into several
LBs using selecting and gating circuits. The reference layout
example is presented in Fig. 6, where every 2 ANDs in
sequences are arranged at the same horizontal level starting
from the top-left corner. Similarly, every 2 TGs in sequences
are placed at the same vertical level starting from the bottom-
left corner. Additional logic gates such as AND gates (AND),
transmission gates (TG), LB column select (LBCS) decoder,
and LB row select (LBRS) decoder are added beyond the Std.
design (Fig. 4). Moreover, the isolation gaps (ISO) are required
to be inserted in between the logic and SRAM regions.
Therefore, the Conv. divided design leads to an area increment
of up to 30% among the optimized subarrays compared to the
Std. design (Fig. 5). Although the subarray-level PP improves,
the overall PP at macro level may be degraded due to the
consequent larger inter-subarray interconnect and logics
overhead [1, 2].
To achieve the division into several local WL (LWL) and BL
(LBL), the ANDs and TGs are connected to global WL (GWL)
and BL (GBL), respectively. AND is used to generate the LWL
signal with the input of GWL and LBLWL, and TG is the
switch between GBL/GBLB and LBL/LBLB. Each LB is
activated by selecting the corresponding LBCS and LBRS
signals. The number of LBs in WL (LBWL) and BL (LBBL)
determines the number of LB in the WL and BL directions,
respectively. The number of columns (Ncol_LB) and rows
(Nrow_LB) in each LB is calculated by dividing the total number
of column (Ncol) and row (Nrow) in subarray by LBWL and
LBBL, respectively. In this initial work, full swing on GBL and
LBL is assumed, but there is potential to extend it to full swing
GBL and low swing LBL for lower energy designs in the future.

C. AIC divided design (Fig. 3(c))
To overcome the area bottleneck observed in the Conv.
divided subarray design, the AIC divided technique is
introduced for SRAM subarray. The AIC concept entails the
introduction of an additional active layer at BEOL and the
integration of BEOL-compatible and low-temperature growth
(250~450°C) CNFET [6, 7, 8, 9]. Consequently, it brings
significant subarray area savings: less than 5% overhead (Fig.
5) at the FEOL by relocating a portion of the devices to the
BEOL layer. It’s important to emphasize that CNFET is
exclusively utilized for the additional logic gates in the divided
design for specific reasons. Firstly, it aids in managing
fabrication costs [15, 17], thereby preventing an undue
escalation in comparison to the Std. subarray design. Secondly,
while several process and design techniques (i.e., imperfection-
immune paradigm [25]) have been introduced to mitigate
CNFET variability, an assessment of the impact of variability
in A14 is still lacking. For example, targets of chiral
purification of CNTs (demonstrated in [26, 27, 28, 29] and
necessary to achieve low variability in leakage and threshold
voltage) are still unclear in A14. As a result, this study
predominantly concentrates on integrating CNFET into
specific circuit components while excluding SRAM bitcell
array and other peripheral elements. The approach aims to
successfully incorporate CNFET in SRAM AIC divided
subarray design, avoiding excessively high fabrication costs
and operating under less strict variability constraints. Future
research should explore hybrid design strategies integrating
both Si and CNT technologies in the SRAM bitcell array and
its peripheral design. This involves a more in-depth
investigation, specifically focusing on mitigating CNFETs
variability in A14.
In this approach, all the ANDs and TGs used for the divided
techniques are moved to the BEOL active layer and placed on
top of the SRAM bitcell array as depicted in the reference
layout example (Fig. 7). The arrangement consists of placing
every 4 ANDs in sequences at the same horizontal level starting
from the top-left corner, and every 2 TGs in sequences at the
same vertical level starting from the bottom-left corner. Note
that the LBCS and LBRS decoders are retained at the FEOL
due to inadequate space available in the BEOL active layer (Fig.
3(c) and Table I).
Fig. 8 and Table III show the schematic views and physical
parameters of 3D vias assumed in AIC divided design. The

D. SRAM Bitcell Layout Design
Fig. 9 displays the three layout designs of HD A14 6T
NSFET SRAM bitcell for Std. [8], Conv. divided, and AIC
divided subarray designs, each with distinct signal routing
requirements. In all designs, the ground voltage (VSS) is
assumed to be placed at the backside using buried power rail
(BPR) technique [30, 31, 32]. The internal nodes (Q and QB),
supply voltage (VDD), and local signals (BL/LBL, BLB/LBLB,
and WL/LWL) are located at the MINT and M1 layers. The
main difference between the Conv. and AIC designs is the
width of LWL, GBL, and GBLB due to distinct pitch
limitations (Table II and Table III). In Conv. divided design
(Fig. 9(b)), the global signals (GWL and GBL) are connected
from the AND and TG located outside the SRAM bitcell
regions (Fig. 6). This arrangement leads to M1, M2, and M3
being exclusively occupied by LWL, GBL/GBLB, and GWL,
respectively. However, in AIC divided subarray design (Fig.
9(c)), the AND and TG are placed right above the SRAM bitcell
regions (Fig. 7). Therefore, the metal widths in AIC divided
subarray design become narrow since extra spaces are required
for the vertical connections from GBL/GBLB and GWL to the
bitcell compared to the conventional counterpart.

B. Variability
Several key factors such as the variation in dCNT, chirality
(yield of metallic or semiconducting CNTs, density, bandgap),
and doping, play a vital role in shaping the characteristics of
CNFET [17]. These sources of variation add to the traditional
variability encountered in silicon CMOS, which may affect the
7
overall PP results. While existing process and design
techniques for mitigating these variations were well-
demonstrated [16, 27, 28, 29], CNT pitch uniformity and CNT
alignment control at the wafer-scale still represent a key
challenge. However, this discussion goes beyond the primary
focus of this work. It is imperative to extend the investigation
of the variation’s impact on CNFETs associated with the
SRAM AIC divided subarray design in future studies.

EDAP gain, respectively, can already be achieved in Std.
design by simply transitioning from small (128-C×128-R) to
large (1024-C×512-R) subarray size due to less H-tree
contribution (disregarding WFR). Fig. 16 clearly shows that the
inter-subarray interconnect has a decisive influence on the
overall macro-level PPA. To optimize this aspect, choosing a
bigger-size subarray is the preferred approach to reduce the
interconnect overhead. Further macro-level PPA improvement
is still achievable even if they involve a larger subarray-level
PPA overhead, which is relatively less impactful than inter-
subarray interconnect. It is worth noting that increasing Nrow is
more effective for PP improvement compared to Ncol since the
longest wire is on horizontal direction [1, 2, 36].

C. Comparison of Si, FDSOI, and CNT models
In this section, the examination focuses on the comparison
of utilizing A14 Si NSFET [23], 22-nm (N22) fully depleted
silicon-on-insulator (FDSOI) [37], and A14 CNFET [14]
models for realistic SRAM AIC divided subarray design to
highlight the benefits of employing CNFET devices (Fig. 19
and Fig. 20). Over the past few years, several experimental
studies on BEOL-compatible and low-temperature-fabricated
CNFETs for 3D integration have been reported [6, 7, 8, 9],
establishing them as realistic candidates for further 3D
integrated-circuit (IC) development. Additionally,
investigations into low-temperature-fabricated FDSOI as top-
level transistors (below 500°C) for 3D stacking technology
have been conducted including electrical characteristics and
reliability measurements [10, 12, 13]. However, it’s important
to note that the FDSOI device is a planar-type transistor, with
mass production limited to 22 nm by GlobalFoundries until
now [37]. Apparently, the device dimensions of N22 FDSOI
transistors could not align with A14 design rules in this study.

(identical model as SRAM bitcell in this work) [23], regardless
of potential degradation from the low-thermal-budget process,
is also considered as an optimistic upper bound for BEOL
logics in our realistic AIC technology comparison.

validates the inherent advantages of employing CNFET as
BEOL-compatible devices in the AIC divided subarray design,
surpassing the optimistic yet unfeasible upper bound
showcased by the A14 Si NSFET counterpart.

V. CONCLUSION
This study presents a comprehensive DTCO analysis,
establishing the potential of the AIC divided subarray design
integrated with CNFET as BEOL-compatible devices for future
PPA improvements of 3D SRAM data array design. In
interconnect-dominated nodes, macro-level PPA is heavily
influenced by the inter-subarray interconnect, a factor
intricately linked to the subarray size. By scaling up the
subarray size from 128-C×128-R to 1024-C×512-R,
remarkable 11%, 23%, 36%, and 56% of macro-level delay,
energy, area, and EDAP improvements, respectively, are
achieved, albeit with the trade-off of a WFR in the Std. subarray
design. To address this WFR, the Conv. divided subarray
design has been explored, offering an improved WM of 338
mV. However, this enhancement comes at the cost of up to 30%
additional macro-level area overhead. To circumvent this area
penalty and maintain it below 3%, the AIC divided technique
emerges as a promising solution for SRAM subarray design. It
not only further enhances the WM (+99 mV) compared to Conv.
counterparts but also provides up to 65% macro-level EDAP
improvement compared to the Std. counterpart, which can only
employ a relatively smaller but realistic subarray size. This
underscores the potential value of the AIC divided subarray
design in advancing PPA of SRAM cache.
